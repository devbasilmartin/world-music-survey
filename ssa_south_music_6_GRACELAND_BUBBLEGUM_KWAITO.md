# The Story of Southern African Music — Installment 6: Graceland, Bubblegum & the Birth of Kwaito (c. 1985-2000)

These fifteen years take South African music from a global controversy to the sound of a free nation. In 1986 the American Paul Simon recorded 'Graceland' with South African musicians -- Ladysmith Black Mambazo, Ray Phiri's Stimela, the fretless-bass wizard Bakithi Kumalo -- putting mbaqanga and isicathamiya on turntables worldwide, while igniting a fierce debate about breaking the UN cultural boycott of apartheid South Africa. At home, glossy synth-driven BUBBLEGUM pop ruled the townships, and its queen Brenda Fassie became the biggest star the country had ever produced; Yvonne Chaka Chaka's 'Umqombothi' and Lucky Dube's roots reggae carried the message across the continent. Then, with liberation in 1994, the 'born-free' generation invented KWAITO -- house music slowed to a swagger, chanted in township slang -- the first genre wholly of the new South Africa: Arthur Mafokate, Boom Shaka, TKZee, and Mandoza's crossover anthem 'Nkalakatha'. Cross-links: Graceland ties directly to the US survey (Paul Simon); kwaito's parent is Chicago/US house filtered through the UK; and it rhymes with the coming West African Afrobeats. Content note: the Graceland boycott-breaking debate, the reclaiming of a racial slur in early kwaito, and the deaths of Brenda Fassie (2004) and the murdered Lucky Dube (2007) are kept with context. Region: all listed artists are South African; Paul Simon is credited as the US cross-link only.

## GRACELAND & THE GLOBAL SPOTLIGHT (1986)

Paul Simon's 'Graceland' (a US-survey album, not listed here) was built on South African players and put their music before the world -- controversially, since it broke the cultural boycott. The South African artists it spotlighted are the story here: Ladysmith Black Mambazo reached a global audience, Ray Phiri's band Stimela was already a township giant, and Bakithi Kumalo's bass became world-famous. (The Boyoyo Boys' 'Puleng' was the mbaqanga instrumental that seeded 'Gumboots'.)

## BUBBLEGUM: BRENDA FASSIE & THE TOWNSHIP POP OF THE 1980s

Bubblegum was fizzy, synth-and-drum-machine township disco, and Brenda Fassie -- 'MaBrrr', the self-styled queen -- was its supernova, from the teen smash 'Weekend Special' to the mature gospel-house of 'Vulindlela' and the defiant 'Black President' (for Mandela). Yvonne Chaka Chaka's 'Umqombothi' became a pan-African anthem, and producer-star Chicco scored the anti-war 'Papa Stop the War'. Content note: Brenda Fassie's turbulent life and 2004 death.

## LUCKY DUBE & SOUTH AFRICAN REGGAE (c. 1987-2000)

Lucky Dube abandoned mbaqanga for Jamaican roots reggae and became Africa's biggest reggae star, singing hard truths about apartheid and unity in English and Zulu. Content note: he was shot dead in a 2007 carjacking in front of his children. Cross-link: this is the reggae of the coming Caribbean survey, re-rooted in Johannesburg.

- Slave — Lucky Dube
- Prisoner — Lucky Dube
- Together as One — Lucky Dube
- Different Colours — Lucky Dube

## THE BIRTH OF KWAITO (post-1994)

Kwaito was the sound of freedom: house music slowed to around 100 bpm, drenched in township slang and attitude, made by and for the Mandela-era 'born-free' youth. Arthur Mafokate's 1995 'Kaffir' -- which confronted and reclaimed the apartheid slur ('don't call me kaffir') -- is often called the first kwaito hit; Boom Shaka (with Lebo Mathosa) and Trompies defined its look and swagger. Content note: the 'Kaffir' title is the real 1995 song and its slur is the point -- an act of reclamation, kept in context, not sanitized.

- It's About Time — Boom Shaka
- Sweetie Lavo — Trompies

## KWAITO GOES BIG: TKZEE, MANDOZA & THE LATE 1990s

By the late 1990s kwaito was the mainstream. TKZee fused it with pop and R&B and soundtracked Bafana Bafana's football fever with 'Shibobo'; Mandoza's 'Nkalakatha' (2000) was a rock-kwaito anthem that crossed every racial line in the new South Africa; and Bongo Maffin and Mafikizolo pointed the way to a slicker Afropop future.

## THE HOUSE ROOTS & THE STAGE MUSICALS (context)

Two currents underlie the era. South Africa fell in love with imported Chicago/US HOUSE, which -- slowed down -- became kwaito's direct parent and the seed of the amapiano to come (Installment 7). And the township MUSICAL went global: Mbongeni Ngema's 'Sarafina!', about the 1976 Soweto uprising, ran on Broadway and became a film. Cross-link: US house (Chicago) and the US survey's stage tradition.
