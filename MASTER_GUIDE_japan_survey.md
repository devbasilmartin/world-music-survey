# The Story of Japanese Music — Master Guide

*A chronological, listenable survey of Japanese music — from gagaku and the shakuhachi to enka, City
Pop, anime, and the global streaming wave — across five installments.*

Japanese music joins an ancient aesthetic of space, restraint, and exquisite timbre to one of the
world's most inventive modern pop cultures. This guide stitches the five installments into one arc: a
tradition rooted in *ma* (charged silence), *wabi-sabi*, and *yugen* that met the West head-on in the
Meiji era and became, across a turbulent century, a global powerhouse of pop, rock, anime, and
virtual-idol music.

The full deduplicated listening list is `MASTER_JAPAN_FULL_SURVEY_IMPORT.txt` — **209 tracks**, zero
cross-installment duplicates. Each installment also has its own `.md` and `_IMPORT.txt`.

## The five installments

**1 — Classical & Traditional Roots (the deep tradition).** `japan_music_1_CLASSICAL_TRADITIONAL_ROOTS`
(45). **Gagaku** (the ancient court orchestra); shomyo chant; the **koto**, **shakuhachi**, **shamisen**,
and biwa; **noh, kabuki & bunraku** theater music; min'yo folk (and Okinawan sanshin); and the
aesthetics of *ma* and *yugen*.

**2 — The Modern Turn: Meiji, Ryukoka & the Roots of Enka (c. 1868-1945).**
`japan_music_2_MEIJI_RYUKOKA_ENKA_ROOTS` (38). The Meiji Westernization and shoka school songs; the
first Western-style composers (Rentaro Taki's "Kojo no Tsuki"); the doyo children's songs; **ryukoka**
and Tokyo's jazz age; the roots of enka; and wartime song.

**3 — The Postwar Boom: Kayokyoku, Enka, Group Sounds & Folk (c. 1945-1975).**
`japan_music_3_POSTWAR_KAYOKYOKU_GS_FOLK` (40). Postwar kayokyoku and the boogie; **Hibari Misora**;
Kyu Sakamoto's "Sukiyaki" (a US Billboard #1); **enka**'s golden age; mood kayo; the **Group Sounds**
rock boom; and the folk boom (Yosui Inoue, Kaguyahime).

**4 — New Music, City Pop & the Idol Age (c. 1975-1995).** `japan_music_4_NEWMUSIC_CITYPOP_IDOLS` (41).
The New Music singer-songwriters (Yuming, Southern All Stars); **City Pop** (Tatsuro Yamashita, Mariya
Takeuchi's "Plastic Love") and its global revival; **YMO** and technopop; the **idol boom** (Seiko
Matsuda, Akina Nakamori, Onyanko Club); and the late-80s Band Boom.

**5 — Modern & Global: J-pop, Anime, Vocaloid & the Wave (c. 1995-present).**
`japan_music_5_JPOP_ANIME_VOCALOID_GLOBAL` (45). The 90s **J-pop** explosion (Komuro, **Hikaru Utada**,
B'z, Mr. Children); **Studio Ghibli**/Joe Hisaishi, anisong and game music; **J-rock & Visual Kei** (X
Japan, Babymetal); **Vocaloid**/Hatsune Miku; the AKB48 idol era; and today's global wave (Kenshi
Yonezu, Yoasobi, Ado).

## Through-lines

- **An aesthetic of space and timbre.** From the single shakuhachi note to City Pop's polish, Japanese
  music prizes *ma* (silence), subtlety of tone, and craftsmanship — a sensibility that persists even in
  the loudest pop and has shaped global ambient and minimalism.
- **Absorbing the West on its own terms.** The Meiji shoka, postwar boogie, City Pop's AOR, Visual Kei's
  metal, Utada's R&B — Japan repeatedly takes in Western music and remakes it into something unmistakably
  Japanese.
- **Tradition ↔ pop, never fully severed.** Enka carries the old melancholy into the pop age; anime
  scores wed orchestral romance to Japanese melody; even Vocaloid descends from a culture of craft.
- **Japan as a music exporter.** "Sukiyaki" in 1963, YMO's global electronics, the City Pop revival,
  and — above all — anime, games, and streaming (Yonezu, Yoasobi, Ado) have made Japanese music natively
  global, cross-linking into the US, Western, and worldwide pop surveys.
- **The invention of new pop forms.** The manufactured idol (and its AKB mega-group model) and the
  Vocaloid virtual idol are Japanese innovations that reshaped how pop is made and consumed worldwide.

## Notes on method & scope

Named entries cite a canonical piece/song and performer; **bare titles** mark forms, instruments, or
artists best used as search seeds (the roots installments lean on these; the pop installments are
named-heavy). All entries are transliterated (romaji), ASCII-only. Deduplication is by **title only**, so
every title within the sub-region is unique. Scope: Japan; China and Korea are separate East Asia
surveys, and Okinawan music is included here as a distinct southern branch.
