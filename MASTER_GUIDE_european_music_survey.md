# The Story of European Music — Complete Master Guide

A chronological survey of how European music evolved, from Gregorian chant (c. 800) to modern
EDM and global pop. **403 tracks, ~1,200 years, one continuous story** — and a deliberate companion
to the American survey.

**How this differs from the American story:** America's is one fusion tradition. Europe's is
**the deepest continuous lineage of notated art music on earth, running in parallel with dozens of
regional folk traditions**, the two constantly cross-pollinating — until the 20th century, when
American forms (jazz, rock, hip-hop, house) wash back across the Atlantic and Europe absorbs and
re-exports them in its own accent. The two surveys meet in the middle: the British Invasion is
American blues coming home transformed; Kraftwerk's electronic pop seeds American hip-hop; the EDM
DJs pulled from the US survey live in the European one.

## The Arc (continuous narrative)

**Medieval & Renaissance (c. 800–1600).** Western notated music begins in the church with plainchant,
then invents its defining achievement: **polyphony** (many independent voices at once) — from
Pérotin at Notre-Dame to Josquin to Palestrina's perfect cathedral sound. Alongside runs the secular
world: troubadours, the first named composer (Hildegard von Bingen), madrigals, and lute songs.

**Baroque (1600–1750).** Music gains its modern grammar: **opera** is invented in Florence, **tonal
harmony** (major/minor keys) becomes the universal language, and the **concerto** makes instrumental
virtuosity an art (Vivaldi). It climaxes in the twin summits of **Bach** (counterpoint perfected) and
**Handel** (public, grand, cosmopolitan).

**Classical (1750–1820).** Clarity and balance after Baroque density. **Haydn** builds the
architecture (symphony, string quartet, sonata form); **Mozart** makes it sing; **Beethoven** blows
it open and drags music single-handedly into Romanticism.

**Romantic (1815–1910).** Music goes maximally emotional. The Lied (Schubert), the piano poets
(Chopin, Liszt), Italian opera (Verdi), and the era-defining rivalry of **Wagner** (radical music-
drama, cracking tonality open) vs. **Brahms** (pure absolute music). Then **nationalism** — composers
building distinct national voices from folk material (the Russian Five, Tchaikovsky, Dvořák, Grieg,
Sibelius) — and the **late-Romantic** orchestra pushed to its absolute limit (Mahler, Strauss).

**Modernism & the birth of pop (1900–1945).** The pivot. In the concert hall, a thousand years of
rules get demolished: Debussy dissolves harmony, Stravinsky weaponizes rhythm (and starts a riot),
Schoenberg abolishes tonality. **At the same time, commercial pop becomes an industry** — operetta,
cabaret, Weimar's savage political song (Weill/Brecht), and American jazz reinvented in a European
accent (Django, Piaf). From here, art music and pop run side by side.

**Postwar avant-garde (1945–2000).** The concert-hall stream goes to extremes: total serialism
(Boulez, Stockhausen), the **invention of electronic music** (Schaeffer, Cologne, the BBC Workshop —
ancestor of all dance music), texture composition (Ligeti), then a swing back to beauty with "holy
minimalism" (Pärt, Górecki). The European film score (Morricone, Rota) bridges art and pop.

**Pop & rock (1950s–70s).** National pop (chanson, San Remo) gives way to the **British Invasion**
(the biggest event in European pop — American rock fired back to conquer the world), art-rock and
prog, and — most consequentially — **Kraftwerk inventing electronic pop**, the seed of disco, synth-
pop, hip-hop, techno, and house. ABBA perfects the pop single.

**Pop (1980s–present).** The reversal: for centuries Europe absorbed American forms; now **Europe
exports the world's pop.** Synth-pop (Depeche Mode, New Order), the rave explosion (Europe turns
American house/techno into a mass movement), Britpop, the French touch and trip-hop, and the modern
era where Swedish production and European EDM DJs supply the global pop machine — the transatlantic
loop, closed.
