# The Story of Sahelian Music — Master Guide

*A chronological, listenable survey of the Sahel's music — the desert-edge belt from Mauritania across
Mali, Niger, and Chad to northern Nigeria — across four installments, from the deep tradition to today.*

The Sahel is where the Sahara meets the savanna, and its music is where North meets South: pentatonic,
modal, and Islam-soaked, carried by hereditary praise-musicians and shaped by empire and caravan. This
is the land that gave the world **desert blues** — and, arguably, a taproot of the blues itself. This
guide stitches the four installments into one arc: from the deep tradition (Moorish, Tuareg, Songhai,
Fulani, Hausa) through Mali's modern orchestral foundation and the birth of desert blues, its global
breakout, and finally crisis, resistance, and a new generation.

Scope note: this survey is the **desert-edge thread** (Mauritania, northern/eastern Mali, Niger, Chad,
northern Nigeria). The coastal **Mande griot/kora world** is covered in the separate, complete West
Africa survey; cross-over figures (Salif Keita, Toumani Diabaté, Habib Koité, Bassekou Kouyaté, Sidiki
Diabaté) are flagged wherever they appear.

The full deduplicated listening list is `MASTER_SSA_SAHEL_FULL_SURVEY_IMPORT.txt` — **175 tracks**, zero
cross-installment duplicates. Each installment also has its own `.md` and `_IMPORT.txt`.

## The four installments

**1 — Desert & Savanna Roots (c. pre-1970).** `ssa_sahel_music_1_DESERT_SAVANNA_ROOTS` (44). The deep
tradition: Mauritania's **Moorish azawan** (tidinit, ardin, iggawin); **Tuareg** imzad, tende, and
assouf; **Songhai** takamba of the Niger bend; **Fulani** flutes and the Wodaabe gerewol; **Hausa** court
trumpets and goje; and the family of **Sahelian lutes** (ngoni, tehardent) whose pentatonic,
call-and-response spirit is an ancestor of the banjo and the blues.

**2 — The Modern Malian Foundation & the Birth of Desert Blues (c. 1960-1990).**
`ssa_sahel_music_2_MALIAN_FOUNDATION_DESERT_BLUES` (43). Independence-era **Radio Mali** and the state
**orchestras** (the Rail Band, Super Biton); **Ali Farka Touré**'s river blues; **Boubacar Traoré**'s
"Mali Twist"; Zani Diabaté's electric guitar; and, in the exile camps, the Tuareg **ishumar** turn that
birthed **Tinariwen**.

**3 — The Global Desert-Blues & Wassoulou Breakout (c. 1990-2010).**
`ssa_sahel_music_3_GLOBAL_DESERT_BLUES_WASSOULOU` (44). Ali Farka Touré's Grammys and the Toumani duets;
**Tinariwen** goes global and desert blues becomes a world genre; **Oumou Sangaré** and the **wassoulou**
divas; **Amadou & Mariam**'s global pop; Habib Koité; Boubacar Traoré's rediscovery; and the **Festival
au Désert** at Timbuktu.

**4 — Crisis, the New Generation & the Sahel Today (c. 2010-present).**
`ssa_sahel_music_4_CRISIS_NEW_GENERATION_TODAY` (44). The 2012 jihadist **music ban** in northern Mali;
**Songhoy Blues** and the music in exile; the **Niger guitar revolution** (Bombino, Mdou Moctar); the
Tuareg new wave (Tamikrest, Les Filles de Illighadad); **Fatoumata Diawara**; Bamako's ngoni masters,
kora-pop, and rap; Mauritania's Noura Mint Seymali; and music as resistance through coups and crisis.

## Through-lines

- **The blues came from here.** The Sahel's pentatonic, plucked-lute, call-and-response tradition —
  ngoni to banjo, Ali Farka Touré to John Lee Hooker — is a documented African root of the American
  blues. It is the survey's central cross-link to the US survey, made explicit again and again.
- **Assouf: from imzad to psych-rock.** The Tuareg music of longing runs from the women's one-string
  fiddle (Installment 1) through the exile-camp electric guitar (Tinariwen, #2) to a global genre
  (#3) and a psych-rock revolution (Bombino, Mdou Moctar, #4) — one unbroken thread of desert blues.
- **Islam and the griot.** Two forces shape everything: Islam (praise, Sufi devotion, and the ban's
  brutal inversion of it in 2012) and the hereditary praise-musician, keeper of memory.
- **Women's voices.** From the Moorish women's ardin and the Wodaabe judging to the wassoulou divas
  (Oumou Sangaré) and today's Fatoumata Diawara and Les Filles de Illighadad, women carry a distinct,
  increasingly assertive Sahelian tradition.
- **Music against erasure.** The arc ends where music becomes literal resistance — banned, exiled, and
  defiantly carried on. The Sahel's music is its memory, and it refuses to be silenced.
