# The Story of European Music — Installment 6: Modernism & the Birth of Pop (c. 1900-1945)

The pivot point: around 1900 the story STOPS being just classical + folk and splits into
parallel worlds. In the concert hall, MODERNISM demolishes the rules -- Debussy dissolves
harmony, Stravinsky weaponizes rhythm (and starts a riot), Schoenberg abolishes tonality
outright. At the SAME time, commercial POPULAR MUSIC becomes an industry: Viennese operetta,
Parisian cabaret, music hall, the savage Weimar cabaret of Weill/Brecht, and American JAZZ
washing in and getting reinvented (Django's Gypsy jazz, Piaf's chanson). From here, both
streams -- avant-garde art music AND pop -- run side by side, exactly like the American survey.

## IMPRESSIONISM: DISSOLVING HARMONY (France, c. 1900-1920)

Debussy and Ravel break from German Romanticism toward color, atmosphere, and ambiguity -- whole-tone scales, unresolved chords, shimmering texture over drama. Harmony stops being about tension-and-resolution and becomes about pure sound. The first great loosening of the tonal system.

- Clair de Lune — Claude Debussy
- Prelude to the Afternoon of a Faun — Claude Debussy
- La Mer: Dialogue of the Wind and the Sea — Claude Debussy
- Bolero — Maurice Ravel
- Pavane pour une infante defunte — Maurice Ravel
- Gymnopedie No. 1 — Erik Satie

## THE MODERNIST EXPLOSION: RHYTHM & THE END OF TONALITY (c. 1910-1925)

Two bombs go off. Stravinsky's 'Rite of Spring' (1913) unleashes savage, pounding rhythm and causes a literal riot at its premiere. And in Vienna, Schoenberg abandons tonality entirely, then invents the 12-tone system -- a total break with the harmonic language that had governed Western music for 300 years.

- The Rite of Spring: Introduction and Augurs of Spring — Igor Stravinsky
- The Firebird: Infernal Dance — Igor Stravinsky
- Petrushka: Russian Dance — Igor Stravinsky
- Verklarte Nacht (Transfigured Night) — Arnold Schoenberg
- Pierrot Lunaire: Mondestrunken — Arnold Schoenberg
- Six Bagatelles for String Quartet — Anton Webern
- Violin Concerto — Alban Berg

## NATIONALISM 2.0 & THE MODERN FOLK SYNTHESIS (c. 1905-1940)

A new generation goes back to the actual field-recorded folk music of their lands and fuses it with modernist technique. Bartok and Kodaly (Hungary), Vaughan Williams and Holst (England), Falla (Spain), and Janacek (Czech) make something both ancient and radically new.

- Romanian Folk Dances — Bela Bartok
- Concerto for Orchestra: Finale — Bela Bartok
- The Lark Ascending — Ralph Vaughan Williams
- The Planets: Jupiter — Gustav Holst
- The Planets: Mars, the Bringer of War — Gustav Holst
- Nights in the Gardens of Spain — Manuel de Falla
- Sinfonietta: Fanfare — Leos Janacek

## NEOCLASSICISM & THE SOVIET VOICE (c. 1920-1945)

Between the wars, some composers pull back from chaos toward clarity and irony (neoclassicism), while in the USSR, Prokofiev and Shostakovich write brilliant, often coded music under the pressures of Stalinist control -- accessible on the surface, dangerous underneath.

- Symphony No. 1 (Classical): Gavotte — Sergei Prokofiev
- Romeo and Juliet: Dance of the Knights — Sergei Prokofiev
- Peter and the Wolf — Sergei Prokofiev
- Symphony No. 5: Finale — Dmitri Shostakovich
- Waltz No. 2 (Jazz Suite) — Dmitri Shostakovich
- Pines of Rome: The Pines of the Appian Way — Ottorino Respighi
- Carmina Burana: O Fortuna — Carl Orff

## OPERETTA, MUSIC HALL & THE BIRTH OF EUROPEAN POP (c. 1890-1930)

The commercial popular-music world explodes in parallel with the concert hall: Viennese operetta, Parisian cabaret, and British/French music hall become mass entertainment -- light, tuneful, theatrical songs sold as sheet music and performed in packed halls. This is European show business being born.

- The Merry Widow: Waltz — Franz Lehar
- Die Fledermaus: Overture — Johann Strauss II
- The Blue Danube — Johann Strauss II
- Under the Bridges of Paris (Sous les ponts de Paris) — (Traditional)
- Ta-ra-ra Boom-de-ay (music hall) — (Traditional)
- Funiculi, Funicula — Luigi Denza

## WEIMAR CABARET & THE POLITICAL SONG (Germany, c. 1920-1933)

Weimar Berlin produces one of the sharpest popular-music moments in history: Kurt Weill and Bertolt Brecht fuse cabaret, jazz, and biting political theater into songs that are catchy and savage at once -- the sound of a society dancing on the edge of catastrophe.

- The Threepenny Opera: Mack the Knife (Die Moritat von Mackie Messer) — Kurt Weill
- The Threepenny Opera: Pirate Jenny — Kurt Weill
- Alabama Song (Whisky Bar) — Kurt Weill
- Falling in Love Again (Ich bin von Kopf bis Fuss) — Marlene Dietrich
- Das Lila Lied (The Lavender Song) — (Traditional)

## EUROPEAN JAZZ & THE CHANSON (c. 1925-1945)

American jazz washes across the Atlantic and Europe reinvents it in its own accent -- above all Gypsy jazz, where Django Reinhardt and Stephane Grappelli create a wholly European swing. Meanwhile the French chanson tradition crystallizes around singers like Piaf and Trenet.

- Minor Swing — Django Reinhardt and Stephane Grappelli
- Nuages — Django Reinhardt
- Djangology — Django Reinhardt
- La Vie en Rose — Edith Piaf
- Non, je ne regrette rien — Edith Piaf
- La Mer — Charles Trenet
- Lili Marleen — Lale Andersen
