# The Story of European Music — Installment 2: The Baroque (c. 1600-1750)

The Baroque is where Western music gains its modern grammar. Throughlines: (1) the birth of
OPERA and expressive solo song (monody) in Italy; (2) TONAL HARMONY -- the major/minor key
system -- becoming the universal language; (3) the invention of the CONCERTO and virtuoso
instrumental music (Vivaldi, Corelli); all climaxing in the twin summits of BACH (counterpoint
perfected, deep and sacred) and HANDEL (public, grand, cosmopolitan). Distinct national styles
-- Italian, French, English, German -- crystallize, and folk traditions run alongside throughout.

## THE BIRTH OF OPERA & MONODY (Italy, c. 1600-1650)

The Baroque begins with a revolution in Florence: a group of intellectuals rejects dense polyphony for a single expressive vocal line over bass (monody), aiming to move the emotions like the ancient Greeks. The result is OPERA -- and Monteverdi, straddling Renaissance and Baroque, becomes its first great master.

- L'Orfeo: Toccata — Claudio Monteverdi
- L'Orfeo: Possente spirto — Claudio Monteverdi
- L'incoronazione di Poppea: Pur ti miro — Claudio Monteverdi
- Lamento della ninfa — Claudio Monteverdi
- Vespro della Beata Vergine: Dixit Dominus — Claudio Monteverdi

## NATIONAL BAROQUE STYLES CRYSTALLIZE (mid-late 1600s)

Distinct national schools emerge. France builds a grand courtly style under Louis XIV (Lully's opera and ballet, Charpentier); England produces its own genius in Purcell; and the German lands develop a deep sacred tradition (Schutz, Buxtehude) that Bach will inherit. Tonal harmony -- major/minor keys -- is becoming the language of Western music.

- Te Deum: Prelude — Marc-Antoine Charpentier
- Armide: Passacaille — Jean-Baptiste Lully
- Dido and Aeneas: Dido's Lament (When I Am Laid in Earth) — Henry Purcell
- Rondeau (Abdelazer) — Henry Purcell
- Music for the Funeral of Queen Mary — Henry Purcell
- Membra Jesu Nostri — Dieterich Buxtehude

## THE ITALIAN INSTRUMENTAL REVOLUTION: THE CONCERTO (c. 1680-1740)

Italy invents the modern concerto -- a soloist (or group) against an orchestra -- and the violin becomes a virtuoso voice. Corelli codifies the form, Vivaldi explodes it into hundreds of vivid, programmatic works, and the ritornello structure they build becomes the engine of Baroque instrumental music.

- The Four Seasons: Spring — Antonio Vivaldi
- The Four Seasons: Winter — Antonio Vivaldi
- Concerto for 2 Cellos in G minor, RV 531 — Antonio Vivaldi
- Gloria in D major: Gloria in excelsis Deo — Antonio Vivaldi
- Concerto Grosso Op. 6 No. 8 (Christmas Concerto) — Arcangelo Corelli
- Canon in D — Johann Pachelbel
- Adagio in G minor — Tomaso Albinoni

## BACH: THE SUMMIT OF THE BAROQUE (1685-1750)

Johann Sebastian Bach synthesized every Baroque national style and pushed counterpoint to a perfection never equaled -- sacred cantatas and passions of overwhelming depth, keyboard works that map the entire tonal system, and instrumental music of endless invention. For many, the greatest composer who ever lived, and the foundation every later composer studied.

- Toccata and Fugue in D minor, BWV 565 — Johann Sebastian Bach
- Brandenburg Concerto No. 3 in G major — Johann Sebastian Bach
- Air on the G String (Suite No. 3) — Johann Sebastian Bach
- Cello Suite No. 1 in G major: Prelude — Johann Sebastian Bach
- Mass in B minor: Kyrie — Johann Sebastian Bach
- St. Matthew Passion: Erbarme dich — Johann Sebastian Bach
- Goldberg Variations: Aria — Johann Sebastian Bach
- Jesu, Joy of Man's Desiring — Johann Sebastian Bach
- The Well-Tempered Clavier: Prelude No. 1 in C major — Johann Sebastian Bach

## HANDEL & THE COSMOPOLITAN BAROQUE (1685-1759)

Bach's exact contemporary took the opposite path -- a cosmopolitan international star who conquered Italian opera and then invented the English oratorio for the public concert hall. Handel's grand, tuneful, public music (and Telemann's prolific fluency) points toward the coming Classical era.

- Messiah: Hallelujah Chorus — George Frideric Handel
- Messiah: And the Glory of the Lord — George Frideric Handel
- Zadok the Priest — George Frideric Handel
- Water Music: Alla Hornpipe — George Frideric Handel
- Music for the Royal Fireworks: La Rejouissance — George Frideric Handel
- Rinaldo: Lascia ch'io pianga — George Frideric Handel
- Sarabande in D minor — George Frideric Handel
