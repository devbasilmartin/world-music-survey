# A Survey of American Music — The 1960s, Strand by Strand

The decade of maximum density. Every stream from your 1890-1960 deep dive comes back
electrified and cross-pollinated. Read the section intros in order and you'll see the logic:
gospel+blues go mainstream as soul/Motown; folk becomes protest poetry and then folk-rock;
Britain sells American blues back to America and forces rock to level up; psychedelia turns
rock into album-art; and James Brown ends the decade by inventing funk, which sets up the 70s.

## MOTOWN: THE SOUND OF YOUNG AMERICA (Detroit)

Berry Gordy ran Motown like an assembly line and did something no Black-owned business had: sold Black music to white teenage America on a massive scale, with crossover pop polish. The house band (the Funk Brothers) and the Holland-Dozier-Holland writing team made it a hit factory. This is soul engineered for the charts.

- You Can't Hurry Love — The Supremes
- My Girl — The Temptations
- Ain't Too Proud to Beg — The Temptations
- Dancing in the Street — Martha and the Vandellas
- My Guy — Mary Wells
- The Tracks of My Tears — Smokey Robinson and The Miracles
- I Heard It Through the Grapevine — Marvin Gaye
- Ain't No Mountain High Enough — Marvin Gaye and Tammi Terrell
- Uptight (Everything's Alright) — Stevie Wonder
- Baby Love — The Supremes
- Reach Out I'll Be There — Four Tops
- I Want You Back — The Jackson 5

## SOUTHERN SOUL: STAX & MUSCLE SHOALS (Memphis / Alabama)

The grittier, church-rooted counterweight to Motown's polish. Recorded live with integrated house bands (Booker T. & the M.G.'s), Stax and Muscle Shoals kept the gospel rawness. This is soul with the sweat left in.

- (Sittin' On) The Dock of the Bay — Otis Redding
- Try a Little Tenderness — Otis Redding
- In the Midnight Hour — Wilson Pickett
- Mustang Sally — Wilson Pickett
- Soul Man — Sam & Dave
- Hold On, I'm Comin' — Sam & Dave
- Green Onions — Booker T. & the M.G.'s
- Knock on Wood — Eddie Floyd
- When a Man Loves a Woman — Percy Sledge

## THE GOSPEL-TO-SOUL CROSSOVER: THE VOICES

The individual titans who carried gospel technique into secular music and defined 'soul' as a vocal art. Sam Cooke left gospel to invent smooth soul (and wrote the movement's anthem); Aretha, a preacher's daughter, became the Queen.

- A Change Is Gonna Come — Sam Cooke
- Bring It On Home to Me — Sam Cooke
- Cupid — Sam Cooke
- Respect — Aretha Franklin
- (You Make Me Feel Like) A Natural Woman — Aretha Franklin
- Chain of Fools — Aretha Franklin
- What'd I Say — Ray Charles
- Georgia on My Mind — Ray Charles
- I Got You (I Feel Good) — James Brown
- Please, Please, Please — James Brown

## THE FOLK REVIVAL & PROTEST (Greenwich Village)

The Woody Guthrie/Lead Belly songbook you just met gets picked up by college kids and turned into the soundtrack of the civil rights and antiwar movements. Then Dylan detonates it — first as protest poetry, then by going electric in 1965, fusing folk lyricism with rock and inventing the singer-songwriter as auteur.

- Blowin' in the Wind — Bob Dylan
- The Times They Are a-Changin' — Bob Dylan
- Like a Rolling Stone — Bob Dylan
- Subterranean Homesick Blues — Bob Dylan
- If I Had a Hammer — Peter, Paul and Mary
- Blowin' in the Wind (cover) — Peter, Paul and Mary
- The Sound of Silence — Simon & Garfunkel
- Turn! Turn! Turn! — The Byrds
- Mr. Tambourine Man — The Byrds
- We Shall Overcome — Mahalia Jackson

## THE BRIT INVASION & THE AMERICAN ANSWER

British bands (Beatles, Stones, Kinks) studied American blues, R&B, and rockabilly, then sold it back to the US in 1964 and conquered the charts. The shock forced American rock to raise its game — the Byrds (folk-rock), the Beach Boys (studio art-pop), and a coming garage/psych wave were all responding to the challenge.

- I Want to Hold Your Hand — The Beatles
- (I Can't Get No) Satisfaction — The Rolling Stones
- You Really Got Me — The Kinks
- California Girls — The Beach Boys
- God Only Knows — The Beach Boys
- Good Vibrations — The Beach Boys
- Wouldn't It Be Nice — The Beach Boys
- Louie Louie — The Kingsmen
- Gloria — The Shadows of Knight

## GIRL GROUPS & THE BRILL BUILDING (early 60s pop craft)

Before the Beatles, teen pop was ruled by producers and professional songwriters (Brill Building) writing three-minute dramas for girl groups. Phil Spector's 'Wall of Sound' turned these into mini-symphonies. This is the pop-craft high-water mark that everyone else measured against.

- Be My Baby — The Ronettes
- Then He Kissed Me — The Crystals
- Will You Love Me Tomorrow — The Shirelles
- Chapel of Love — The Dixie Cups
- Leader of the Pack — The Shangri-Las
- River Deep - Mountain High — Ike & Tina Turner
- He's a Rebel — The Crystals

## SURF & INSTRUMENTAL ROCK (California)

The guitar-forward, reverb-drenched sound of Southern California car-and-beach culture. Dick Dale's Middle Eastern-tinged fast picking and the Beach Boys' harmonies defined an American teenage idyll — and pushed electric-guitar technique forward.

- Miserlou — Dick Dale and His Del-Tones
- Pipeline — The Chantays
- Wipe Out — The Surfaris
- Walk, Don't Run — The Ventures
- Surfin' U.S.A. — The Beach Boys

## PSYCHEDELIA & ACID ROCK (San Francisco, 1966-1969)

The counterculture plugs in and expands. Studio experimentation, extended improvisation, blues-rock virtuosity, and drug-culture imagery turn rock into an 'album art' medium. Hendrix reinvents the electric guitar's vocabulary entirely.

- Purple Haze — The Jimi Hendrix Experience
- Voodoo Child (Slight Return) — The Jimi Hendrix Experience
- All Along the Watchtower — The Jimi Hendrix Experience
- White Rabbit — Jefferson Airplane
- Somebody to Love — Jefferson Airplane
- Light My Fire — The Doors
- Piece of My Heart — Big Brother and the Holding Company
- Truckin' — Grateful Dead
- Born to Be Wild — Steppenwolf

## ROOTS ROCK & THE BLUES REVIVAL

A counter-reaction to psychedelia: American bands going back to blues, country, and swamp roots. CCR distilled American radio-rock; Creedence and the blues-rock revival kept the through-line to the source material visible.

- Proud Mary — Creedence Clearwater Revival
- Fortunate Son — Creedence Clearwater Revival
- Born on the Bayou — Creedence Clearwater Revival
- The Weight — The Band
- Crossroads — Cream

## COUNTRY IN THE 60s: NASHVILLE VS. BAKERSFIELD

Country split in two: Nashville smoothed it out with strings and choirs (the 'Nashville Sound') to compete with pop, while Bakersfield, California pushed back with a hard, twangy, electric honky-tonk edge that would feed outlaw country and even rock.

- Crazy — Patsy Cline
- She Thinks I Still Care — George Jones
- Ring of Fire — Johnny Cash
- Folsom Prison Blues (Live) — Johnny Cash
- Act Naturally — Buck Owens
- Together Again — Buck Owens
- Mama Tried — Merle Haggard
- Coal Miner's Daughter — Loretta Lynn
- Stand by Your Man — Tammy Wynette

## JAZZ PUSHES OUT: MODAL, SOUL-JAZZ & FREE (1960s)

Jazz kept innovating on multiple fronts: Coltrane's spiritual modal explorations, soul-jazz grooves for the jukebox, and Coleman/Coltrane's free jazz breaking tonality entirely — the avant-garde edge of American music.

- A Love Supreme, Pt. 1: Acknowledgement — John Coltrane
- My Favorite Things — John Coltrane
- The Sidewinder — Lee Morgan
- Song for My Father — Horace Silver
- Cantaloupe Island — Herbie Hancock
- Lonely Woman — Ornette Coleman

## THE BIRTH OF FUNK (late 60s)

The hinge into the next era. James Brown stopped thinking in chords and melody and started thinking in interlocking rhythm — 'on the one.' Funk is what happens when soul becomes pure groove, and it's the direct rhythmic ancestor of disco and hip-hop.

- Papa's Got a Brand New Bag — James Brown
- Cold Sweat — James Brown
- Say It Loud - I'm Black and I'm Proud — James Brown
- I Want to Take You Higher — Sly and the Family Stone
- Everyday People — Sly and the Family Stone
- Grazing in the Grass — Hugh Masekela
