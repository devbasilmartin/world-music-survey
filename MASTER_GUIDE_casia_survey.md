# The Story of Central Asian & Caucasian Music — Master Guide

*A chronological, listenable survey of the music of Central Asia and the Caucasus — the Turkic and Mongol
steppe, the Silk Road oasis cities, and the Caucasus mountains — from throat-singing and the epic bards to
the maqam, mugham, polyphony, and the post-Soviet global present, across four installments.*

Between China, Persia, Russia, and the Middle East lies a vast crossroads of empires and peoples, holding
some of the world's most singular music: the two-voiced throat-singing and horse-head fiddle of the
nomadic steppe, the refined maqam and mugham of the Silk Road cities, the ancient vocal polyphony of
Georgia and the plaintive duduk of Armenia — and, after the Soviet century, a modern pop present with
global stars. This guide stitches the four installments into one arc, from the open sky to the global
stage.

The full deduplicated listening list is `MASTER_CASIA_FULL_SURVEY_IMPORT.txt` — **164 tracks**, zero
cross-installment duplicates. Each installment also has its own `.md` and `_IMPORT.txt`.

## The four installments

**1 — The Turkic & Steppe Roots.** `casia_music_1_TURKIC_STEPPE_THROAT_SINGING` (41). **Throat-singing**
(khoomei; Huun-Huur-Tu, Yat-Kha) and its styles (sygyt/kargyraa); the Mongolian **morin khuur** horse-head
fiddle and the **long song**; the Kazakh **dombra** and the **kui** (Kurmangazy); the Kyrgyz **komuz** and
the **Manas** epic (the manaschi); and the steppe bards (akyn, jyrau, bakhshi, the shaman's song).

**2 — Persianate Art Music & the Silk Road.** `casia_music_2_SILK_ROAD_MAQAM_MUGHAM` (41). The shared
**maqam/muqam/mugham** modal idea; Uzbek/Tajik **shashmaqam** (and the Bukharan Jewish transmission); the
Uyghur **on ikki muqam**; Azerbaijani **mugham** (Alim Qasimov); the **ashiq** bard and the saz; Turkmen
**bagshy** and the dutar; the Silk Road instruments; and the Aga Khan / Silk Road Ensemble revival.

**3 — The Caucasus.** `casia_music_3_CAUCASUS_POLYPHONY_DUDUK` (43). Georgian three-part **vocal
polyphony** ("Chakrulo" on Voyager, the Rustavi Choir, the Gurian krimanchuli, the supra); Georgian sacred
chant; the Armenian **duduk** (Djivan Gasparyan) and **Komitas**, who saved Armenian folk song; Armenian
instruments and the diaspora; Azerbaijani folk and the **balaban**; and the North Caucasus **lezginka**.

**4 — The Soviet Century & the Modern Present (Finale).** `casia_music_4_SOVIET_MODERN_POP` (39). Soviet
**estrada** and the state folk ensembles; the VIA era (Yalla); the post-1991 awakening and rock; the
Kazakh global star **Dimash** and the **Q-pop** wave (Ninety One); Azerbaijan at Eurovision; the diaspora
(System of a Down, Katie Melua); and the **folk-fusion** reinvention of the ancient roots.

## Through-lines

- **The music of the open sky.** The nomadic steppe gave the world its most singular sounds — two voices
  from one throat, the horse-head fiddle, the half-million-line Manas epic — a music inseparable from the
  horse, the yurt, and shamanism.
- **The modal soul of the Silk Road.** The maqam, muqam, and mugham are one great family of modes,
  elaborated in the courtly suites of Bukhara, Kashgar, and Baku — cousins to the Persian dastgah and the
  Turkish and Arab maqam.
- **The ancient voice of the Caucasus.** Georgian polyphony is among the oldest on Earth, and the Armenian
  duduk one of the most moving sounds in music — a mountain world of faith, feast, and endurance.
- **The bard and the sung word.** From the steppe akyn and manaschi to the ashiq of Azerbaijan and the
  Armenian ashugh Sayat-Nova, the poet-singer — keeper of memory, epic, and improvised verse — runs
  through the whole region.
- **The Soviet rupture and the global present.** Seventy Soviet years institutionalized, reshaped, and
  suppressed this music; since 1991 the region has reclaimed its roots and sent new stars — Dimash, Q-pop,
  the diaspora — onto the global stage, where the most ancient traditions and the newest pop live side by
  side.

## Notes on method & scope

Named entries cite a canonical song and performer; **bare titles** mark genres, instruments, forms, or
traditions best used as search seeds (the deep-roots installments 1-3 lean heavily on these; the modern
finale is named-heavier). All entries are transliterated, ASCII-only. Deduplication is by **title only**,
so every title within the region is unique. Sensitive material — Soviet cultural policy and Russification,
the 1915 Armenian Genocide and Komitas's silence, the Uyghur situation in Xinjiang, and Caucasus conflicts
— is presented strictly factually, evenhandedly, and with care, as music. Scope notes: the Bukharan Jewish
role in shashmaqam is cross-linked to the planned Jewish-diaspora survey; the Persian, Turkish, and Arab
maqam worlds live in the MENA survey; Uyghur music is included as part of the Turkic-Persianate Silk Road
world.
