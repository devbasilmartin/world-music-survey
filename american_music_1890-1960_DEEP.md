# A Survey of American Music — The Deep Middle (1890-1960)

The period where nearly every American genre was invented. Organized by strand rather than
strict chronology, so you can hear parallel streams evolving and feeding each other.
The engine underneath all of it: African American musical language meeting European song
forms, recombining over and over. Blues+church=gospel. Blues+string band=honky-tonk/rock.
Blues+brass=jazz. And the business moved sheet music -> records -> radio, which is *why*
the music shifted from songs-anyone-plays to performances-only-one-person-delivers.

## TIN PAN ALLEY & THE SHEET-MUSIC ERA (1890s-1910s)

Before records ruled, songs were products you bought as sheet music and played at home. A few blocks of Manhattan publishers ('Tin Pan Alley') manufactured the nation's hits. This is where the modern pop songwriting craft — the 32-bar form, the memorable hook — was industrialized. Note: many of these you already have in your 1890-1920 piano book; here are landmark recordings.

- The Sidewalks of New York — The Haydn Quartet
- Give My Regards to Broadway — Billy Murray
- Yankee Doodle Boy — Billy Murray
- In the Good Old Summertime — Harry Macdonough
- Bill Bailey, Won't You Please Come Home — Arthur Collins
- Meet Me in St. Louis, Louis — Billy Murray
- Shine On, Harvest Moon — Ada Jones and Billy Murray
- By the Light of the Silvery Moon — Billy Murray and Ada Jones
- Alexander's Ragtime Band — Billy Murray
- Let Me Call You Sweetheart — The Peerless Quartet

## RAGTIME (1890s-1917)

Syncopated piano music, born from Black pianists in the Midwest, that became the first African American music to sweep white America. Its 'ragged' rhythm loosened the stiff parlor tradition and set the stage for jazz. Scott Joplin aimed to make it respected art music.

- Maple Leaf Rag — Scott Joplin
- The Entertainer — Scott Joplin
- The Easy Winners — Scott Joplin
- Gladiolus Rag — Scott Joplin
- Grace and Beauty — James Scott
- Charleston Rag — Eubie Blake
- Twelfth Street Rag — Euday L. Bowman

## THE BLUES: COUNTRY/DELTA ROOTS (1920s-1940s)

The blues is the taproot. Emerging from field hollers, work songs, and spirituals in the Deep South, it gave America the 12-bar form, the blue note, and the confessional first-person voice that everything from rock to hip-hop still uses. First the raw solo Delta bluesmen, recorded on 'race records.'

- Cross Road Blues — Robert Johnson
- Hellhound on My Trail — Robert Johnson
- Sweet Home Chicago — Robert Johnson
- Pony Blues — Charley Patton
- Death Letter Blues — Son House
- Statesboro Blues — Blind Willie McTell
- Matchbox Blues — Blind Lemon Jefferson
- See That My Grave Is Kept Clean — Blind Lemon Jefferson
- Spoonful — Charley Patton
- Devil Got My Woman — Skip James

## THE BLUES: CLASSIC / VAUDEVILLE (the women who sold it first, 1920s)

The very first blues records were huge hits by women backed by jazz bands. Mamie Smith's 1920 'Crazy Blues' proved a market for Black music and launched the entire recording industry's 'race records' line. Bessie Smith became the highest-paid Black performer of the '20s.

- Crazy Blues — Mamie Smith
- Downhearted Blues — Bessie Smith
- St. Louis Blues — Bessie Smith
- Nobody Knows You When You're Down and Out — Bessie Smith
- See See Rider Blues — Ma Rainey
- Prove It on Me Blues — Ma Rainey
- Wild Women Don't Have the Blues — Ida Cox

## EARLY JAZZ: NEW ORLEANS TO CHICAGO (1917-1929)

Brass-band tradition + blues + ragtime, improvised. It moved up the river from New Orleans to Chicago and became the sound of the decade ('The Jazz Age'). Louis Armstrong personally invented the idea of the virtuoso soloist and swing phrasing — arguably the most influential musician in American history.

- Livery Stable Blues — Original Dixieland Jass Band
- Dipper Mouth Blues — King Oliver's Creole Jazz Band
- West End Blues — Louis Armstrong and His Hot Five
- Potato Head Blues — Louis Armstrong and His Hot Seven
- Hotter Than That — Louis Armstrong and His Hot Five
- Black Bottom Stomp — Jelly Roll Morton
- Singin' the Blues — Frankie Trumbauer and Bix Beiderbecke
- Struttin' with Some Barbecue — Louis Armstrong

## THE SWING ERA / BIG BANDS (1930s-1940s)

Jazz became America's pop music. Big bands filled ballrooms, the radio carried them nationwide, and dancing to a 'swing' beat was the mainstream. This is the sound of the Depression and WWII home front.

- It Don't Mean a Thing (If It Ain't Got That Swing) — Duke Ellington
- Take the A Train — Duke Ellington
- Sing, Sing, Sing — Benny Goodman
- One O'Clock Jump — Count Basie
- In the Mood — Glenn Miller
- Moonlight Serenade — Glenn Miller
- Cheek to Cheek — Fred Astaire
- Sing, Sing, Sing (with a Swing) — Benny Goodman
- A-Tisket, A-Tasket — Ella Fitzgerald with Chick Webb
- Tuxedo Junction — Erskine Hawkins

## THE GREAT AMERICAN SONGBOOK & SHOW TUNES (1920s-1950s)

The golden age of Broadway and Hollywood songwriting — Gershwin, Porter, Berlin, Kern, Rodgers & Hart. These sophisticated songs became the standard repertoire that every jazz musician and crooner reinterpreted for decades, the shared songbook of American culture.

- Summertime — George Gershwin
- I Got Rhythm — Ethel Merman
- Ol' Man River — Paul Robeson

- Over the Rainbow — Judy Garland

- Blue Skies — Al Jolson
- My Funny Valentine — Chet Baker
- Someone to Watch Over Me — Ella Fitzgerald

## THE CROONERS & VOCAL POP (1930s-1950s)

The microphone changed singing. Instead of belting to the back row, singers could be intimate and conversational — Crosby, then Sinatra, became the first modern pop idols, and the template for the solo star.

- Pennies from Heaven — Bing Crosby
- I'll Be Seeing You — Bing Crosby
- I've Got You Under My Skin — Frank Sinatra
- Nature Boy — Nat King Cole
- Mona Lisa — Nat King Cole
- That Old Black Magic — Frank Sinatra

- Unforgettable — Nat King Cole

## GOSPEL: THE SANCTIFIED SOUND (1930s-1950s)

Thomas A. Dorsey fused blues feeling with sacred lyrics and invented modern gospel; Sister Rosetta Tharpe added electric guitar and basically pre-invented rock and roll; Mahalia Jackson became its transcendent voice. Gospel is the vocal training ground that produced soul and R&B.

- Take My Hand, Precious Lord — Mahalia Jackson
- Move On Up a Little Higher — Mahalia Jackson
- Strange Things Happening Every Day — Sister Rosetta Tharpe
- Up Above My Head — Sister Rosetta Tharpe
- This Train — Sister Rosetta Tharpe
- Peace in the Valley — Red Foley
- Oh Happy Day — The Golden Gate Quartet
- Wade in the Water — The Golden Gate Quartet

## COUNTRY: FROM HILLBILLY TO HONKY-TONK (1920s-1950s)

Recorded country began in 1927 when Ralph Peer taped Jimmie Rodgers and the Carter Family in Bristol, TN — the 'Big Bang' of country. Rodgers (the blue yodel) and the Carters (the family harmony and song-catalog) are the two founding streams. It grew into western swing, bluegrass, and the jukebox heartbreak of honky-tonk, where Hank Williams became country's Shakespeare.

- Wildwood Flower — The Carter Family
- Can the Circle Be Unbroken (Bye and Bye) — The Carter Family

- Blue Moon of Kentucky — Bill Monroe
- Your Cheatin' Heart — Hank Williams
- I'm So Lonesome I Could Cry — Hank Williams
- Lovesick Blues — Hank Williams
- Walking the Floor Over You — Ernest Tubb
- I Walk the Line — Johnny Cash
- Crazy — Patsy Cline

## FOLK & THE PROTEST TRADITION (1930s-1950s)

The Depression and labor movements gave folk a political edge. Woody Guthrie and Lead Belly, recorded by the Lomaxes and Moe Asch, became the conscience-of-the-people songbook that Dylan and the '60s revival would inherit wholesale.

- This Land Is Your Land — Woody Guthrie
- Pastures of Plenty — Woody Guthrie
- Goodnight, Irene — Lead Belly
- The Midnight Special — Lead Belly
- We Shall Overcome — Pete Seeger
- If I Had a Hammer — The Weavers

## ELECTRIC BLUES & JUMP: THE BRIDGE TO ROCK (late 1940s-1950s)

Blues went electric and urban when the Great Migration brought it to Chicago; Muddy Waters plugged in and made it loud. Meanwhile Louis Jordan's small-combo 'jump blues' put a backbeat and a party into it. Put those together and you get rhythm & blues — and rock and roll is one small step away.

- Hoochie Coochie Man — Muddy Waters
- Rollin' Stone — Muddy Waters
- Smokestack Lightnin' — Howlin' Wolf
- Boogie Chillen' — John Lee Hooker
- Call It Stormy Monday — T-Bone Walker
- Choo Choo Ch'Boogie — Louis Jordan
- Saturday Night Fish Fry — Louis Jordan
- The Things That I Used to Do — Guitar Slim
- Rocket 88 — Jackie Brenston and His Delta Cats
- Shake, Rattle and Roll — Big Joe Turner

## BEBOP & MODERN JAZZ (1940s-1950s)

Young players turned jazz from dance music into fast, harmonically dense art music meant for listening. Parker and Gillespie led the revolution; Miles Davis then cooled it down and, by 1959, opened modal jazz — jazz's most influential single decade of innovation.

- Ko-Ko — Charlie Parker
- A Night in Tunisia — Dizzy Gillespie
- Salt Peanuts — Dizzy Gillespie
- 'Round Midnight — Thelonious Monk
- So What — Miles Davis
- Take Five — The Dave Brubeck Quartet
- My Favorite Things — John Coltrane
- Moanin' — Art Blakey and the Jazz Messengers

## DOO-WOP & THE R&B VOCAL GROUPS (1950s)

Street-corner harmony groups blended gospel quartet singing with pop, aimed at teenagers. This is the direct on-ramp to Motown and '60s soul, and the sound that made vocal harmony central to American pop.

- Sh-Boom — The Chords
- Earth Angel — The Penguins
- The Great Pretender — The Platters
- Only You (And You Alone) — The Platters
- Why Do Fools Fall in Love — Frankie Lymon and The Teenagers
- In the Still of the Nite — The Five Satins
- Gee — The Crows

## ROCK 'N' ROLL: THE FUSION IGNITES (1954-1959)

Everything above collides. Country + electric blues + gospel + doo-wop, marketed to a new teenage audience with money and radios. Chuck Berry welds blues to storytelling and guitar; Little Richard brings gospel fire; Elvis carries it to the white mainstream. American music is never the same.
