# The Story of Middle Eastern & North African Music — Master Guide

*A chronological, listenable survey of the music of the MENA world — Arab, Persian, Turkish, and
Maghrebi — from the maqam and the muwashshah to the golden-age divas, rai, and the digital present,
across six installments.*

The music of the Middle East and North Africa is a vast, interlocking family united by a shared love of
the modal system (maqam / makam / dastgah), the sung poem, and the tarab of ecstatic performance — yet
splendidly diverse across its Arab, Persian, Turkish, and Berber worlds. This guide stitches the six
installments into one arc, from the courtly maqam to the Cairo golden age, the Persian and Turkish
classical worlds, the Maghreb's rai and Gnawa, and a pan-Arab, Mizrahi, and digital today.

The full deduplicated listening list is `MASTER_MENA_FULL_SURVEY_IMPORT.txt` — **259 tracks**, zero
cross-installment duplicates. Each installment also has its own `.md` and `_IMPORT.txt`.

## The six installments

**1 — Arab Classical & Maqam Roots.** `mena_music_1_ARAB_CLASSICAL_MAQAM_ROOTS` (44). The **maqam** modal
system and iqa'at rhythms; the **takht** ensemble (oud, qanun, ney); the classical forms (taqsim, dawr,
qasida); the **muwashshah** and Ziryab's Andalusian legacy; **tarab**; and the Nahda (Sayyid Darwish).

**2 — The Egyptian Golden Age & the Great Divas (c. 1920-1975).**
`mena_music_2_EGYPTIAN_GOLDEN_AGE_DIVAS` (40). Cairo as the Arab cultural capital; **Umm Kulthum** ("Enta
Omri", "Al Atlal"); Mohammed Abdel Wahab; Abdel Halim Hafez; Farid al-Atrash & Asmahan; and Lebanon's
**Fairuz** with the Rahbani Brothers.

**3 — Persian (Iranian) Classical & Pop.** `mena_music_3_PERSIAN_CLASSICAL_POP` (44). The **dastgah**
system and the avaz; **Shajarian** ("Morgh-e Sahar"); the golden-age pop divas (Googoosh, Dariush,
Hayedeh); the 1979 rupture; the **Tehrangeles** diaspora; and inside-Iran protest ("Baraye").

**4 — Turkish Music: Ottoman Classical, Arabesk, Anatolian Rock & Pop.**
`mena_music_4_TURKISH_OTTOMAN_AREBESK_ROCK_POP` (43). Ottoman **makam** and the **Mevlevi** Sufi music;
the asik bards (Asik Veysel); Zeki Muren; **arabesk** (Orhan Gencebay); **Anatolian rock** (Baris Manco,
Selda Bagcan); and modern pop (Sezen Aksu, Tarkan).

**5 — The Maghreb: Andalusian, Chaabi, Rai, Gnawa & Amazigh.**
`mena_music_5_MAGHREB_RAI_GNAWA_AMAZIGH` (47). The Andalusian classical legacy; Algerian chaabi ("Ya
Rayah"); **rai** (Cheikha Rimitti → Cheb Khaled's "Didi"); Moroccan **Gnawa** trance; Nass El Ghiwane;
and **Berber/Amazigh** music (Idir's "A Vava Inouva").

**6 — Modern Pan-Arab Pop, Mizrahi & the Region Today (c. 1990-present).**
`mena_music_6_MODERN_PANARAB_MIZRAHI_TODAY` (41). The satellite pan-Arab pop era (**Amr Diab**, Nancy
Ajram, Kadim Al Sahir); **Mizrahi**/Israeli music (Ofra Haza, A-WA); Arab Spring hip-hop; Palestinian
music; and the **mahraganat**/electronic underground.

## Through-lines

- **The modal soul.** The maqam, makam, and dastgah are cousins in one great family — a system of
  microtonal modes, each an emotional world, that underlies classical and pop alike across the region.
- **The sung poem.** From the qasida and the muwashshah to Shajarian singing Hafez, Kadim Al Sahir
  setting Qabbani, and rai's frank verses, MENA music is inseparable from poetry.
- **Tarab and the diva.** The region's genius for the great voice — Umm Kulthum, Fairuz, Googoosh, Zeki
  Muren, Ofra Haza — and for the ecstatic communion of tarab runs through every thread.
- **Al-Andalus, the shared inheritance.** The music of Muslim Spain seeds the muwashshah, the Maghreb's
  classical suites, and — cross-linking to Europe — flamenco itself.
- **Exile, upheaval, and the digital present.** Revolution and censorship (Iran 1979, the Algerian civil
  war, the Arab Spring) scattered artists and made music a form of resistance; satellite TV, streaming,
  and the mahraganat underground carry it into a connected, contested now.

## Notes on method & scope

Named entries cite a canonical song and performer; **bare titles** mark maqamat, forms, instruments, or
artists best used as search seeds (the roots installments lean on these; the golden-age and pop
installments are named-heavy). All entries are transliterated, ASCII-only. Deduplication is by **title
only**, so every title within the region is unique. Sensitive political and religious material (the
revolution, the Arab Spring, Israeli-Mizrahi and Palestinian music, Quranic recitation and Sufi chant)
is presented factually, evenhandedly, and with care — as music. Scope note: the Jewish diaspora has its
own planned survey; Mizrahi music appears here as part of the region's living sound.
