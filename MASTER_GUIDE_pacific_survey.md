# The Story of Pacific & Oceanian Music — Master Guide

*A chronological, listenable survey of the music of Oceania — Aboriginal Australia, Melanesia, Polynesia,
and Micronesia — from the forty-thousand-year songlines and the didgeridoo to the ukulele string band,
island reggae, and modern Aboriginal, Maori, and Pasifika pop, across four installments.*

Oceania holds humanity's oldest living music and its widest ocean. From the songlines of Aboriginal
Australia — the longest continuous musical culture on Earth — through the astonishing diversity of
Melanesia, the chant-and-dance world of Polynesia, and the atolls of Micronesia, the Pacific built a music
of ceremony, drone, drum, and voice. Over the 20th century the ukulele, the hymn, and reggae wove it into
a shared "island music", and today Aboriginal, Maori, and Pasifika artists carry the oldest music on Earth
to the global stage. This guide stitches the four installments into one arc, from the songline to the
global chart.

The full deduplicated listening list is `MASTER_PACIFIC_FULL_SURVEY_IMPORT.txt` — **168 tracks**, zero
cross-installment duplicates. Each installment also has its own `.md` and `_IMPORT.txt`.

## The four installments

**1 — Aboriginal Australia & Melanesia.** `pacific_music_1_ABORIGINAL_MELANESIA` (42). The oldest living
music (the Dreaming); the **didgeridoo/yidaki** and circular breathing; the **songlines**, clapsticks, and
corroboree; the Torres Strait; Melanesia and **Papua New Guinea**'s diversity (kundu, garamut, the
**sing-sing**, the sacred flutes); the **Solomon Islands** panpipes ("Rorogwela"); and Vanuatu
water-drumming.

**2 — Polynesia.** `pacific_music_2_POLYNESIA` (43). The Maori **haka**, waiata, poi, and the revived
**taonga puoro**; the Hawaiian **mele** and **hula**, **slack-key** guitar, falsetto, the steel guitar, the
ukulele, and **Israel Kamakawiwo'ole**; Samoa and Tonga (the slap-dance, the lakalaka); Tahiti and the
Cook Islands (the **'ote'a** drum-dance); and the great Polynesian **church hymnody**.

**3 — Micronesia & the Island-Pop, String-Band & Hymnody Era.** `pacific_music_3_MICRONESIA_ISLAND_POP`
(40). Micronesian chant, stick-dance, and navigation song; the **Chamorro**; the **Marshall Islands** and
the songs of nuclear exile; the **ukulele string band**; the pan-Pacific **himene**/hymn harmony; the birth
of **island reggae** (Jawaiian); and the **Hawaiian renaissance**.

**4 — Modern Pacific & Aboriginal/Maori Pop (Finale).** `pacific_music_4_MODERN_POP` (43). Aboriginal rock
(**Yothu Yindi**'s "Treaty"), **Archie Roach** and the Stolen Generations, **Gurrumul**, Baker Boy; Maori
and Pasifika modern (Herbs, "Poi E", **OMC**'s "How Bizarre", Six60, Stan Walker, te reo pop); **island
reggae** today (Katchafire, J Boog); Pacific and PNG pop; and the Pasifika **diaspora**.

## Through-lines

- **The oldest music on Earth.** Aboriginal Australia's songlines are the longest continuous musical
  tradition known — song as law, map, and memory — and the whole Pacific keeps music at the center of
  ceremony, identity, and survival.
- **Song as country and genealogy.** From the Dreaming to the Hawaiian mele to the Maori waiata, Oceanian
  music encodes the land, the ancestors, and the voyaging history of the world's greatest navigators.
- **The voice, the drum, and the drone.** Chant and rich vocal harmony, the pahu and to'ere drums, and the
  yidaki drone are the deep materials of Pacific music, from the sacred to the festival.
- **The ukulele, the hymn, and island reggae.** Three 20th-century currents — the guitar/ukulele string
  band, the transformed missionary hymn, and Jamaican reggae remade as island reggae — fused into a shared
  "island music" that binds the whole ocean.
- **The oldest meets the newest.** Yothu Yindi's didgeridoo-rock, Baker Boy's Yolngu hip-hop, te reo Maori
  pop, and the global Pasifika diaspora carry the most ancient music into the newest forms, on the global
  stage.

## Notes on method & scope

Named entries cite a canonical song and performer; **bare titles** mark forms, instruments, ceremonies, or
traditions best used as search seeds (the deep-roots installments 1-3 lean heavily on these; the modern
finale is named-heavier). All entries are romanized to plain ASCII (okina and macrons dropped), and
deduplication is by **title only**, so every title within the region is unique. Sensitive material — the
sacred and secret-sacred status of much Aboriginal and Melanesian music, the ethics of recording and
sampling Indigenous music ("Rorogwela"), colonization and the near-loss of the Maori and Hawaiian
languages, US nuclear testing in the Marshall Islands, and the Stolen Generations — is presented factually,
respectfully, and, wherever it is the substance of the music, as the music's own subject. Scope note: some
of this music is restricted by custom and is included only by title as a pointer, not as a claim to
public performance.
