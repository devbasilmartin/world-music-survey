# A Survey of American Music — The 2010s, Strand by Strand

The decade genre boundaries dissolved and streaming rewrote every rule. Throughlines: (1) TRAP
becomes the rhythmic foundation of ALL pop, not just rap; (2) the rap/R&B/singing blur -- Drake
makes 'sung rap' the dominant mode while Frank Ocean and the Weeknd pioneer moody alt-R&B; (3)
the statement-album prestige track (Kendrick, Beyonce) coexisting with pure streaming pop; and
(4) the final collapse of genre itself, symbolized by 'Old Town Road.' EDM crosses over early,
indie merges into pop, and SoundCloud lets anyone bypass the industry entirely.

## TRAP BECOMES THE DEFAULT SOUND OF POP

The Atlanta trap sound -- rattling hi-hats, booming 808s, half-time menace -- stopped being a subgenre and became the rhythmic foundation of nearly all pop and hip-hop. Producers like Metro Boomin and Mike WiLL Made-It set the template; Future, Migos, and Young Thug made it an art of texture and melody.

## KENDRICK & THE RETURN OF THE STATEMENT ALBUM

As trap ruled the clubs, a wave of rappers made dense, ambitious concept albums that won critical prestige and Pulitzers. Kendrick Lamar became the defining lyricist of the decade; J. Cole and others proved rap could be both introspective art and stadium-huge.

## DRAKE & THE MELODIC RAP/R&B BLUR

Drake blurred the line between rapping and singing until it dissolved, making moody, melodic, emotionally confessional hip-hop the dominant mode. This 'sung rap' became the biggest commercial sound of the decade and reshaped what a rapper could be.

## BEYONCE, R&B & THE ALT-R&B WAVE

R&B fractured into a moody, atmospheric, alternative strain (the Weeknd, Miguel, SZA) even as Beyonce ascended to make grand, genre-spanning visual-album statements that fused R&B, hip-hop, rock, and Black cultural politics into event releases.

## EDM CROSSES OVER (the festival-pop boom)

Electronic dance music broke into the American mainstream around 2011-2014, with big-room drops and festival culture (EDM) fueling pop hits. American and European DJs turned dance music into radio pop and the sound of a generation's parties.

- Harlem Shake — Baauer

- Sad Machine — Porter Robinson

## POP GOES ELECTRO, THEN GOES EVERYWHERE

Mainstream pop absorbed EDM's drops and hip-hop's rhythms while a run of superstar women defined the era. From Gaga's electro-theater to Katy Perry's hit machine to Taylor Swift's full pivot from country to pop, this is blockbuster chart pop at its peak.

## THE INDIE-TO-MAINSTREAM PIPELINE & FOLK REVIVAL

Indie broke fully into pop: anthemic indie-rock, a stomp-and-holler folk revival, and synth-driven indie-pop all crossed over to radio. The lines between 'indie' and 'pop' basically vanished by mid-decade.

## POP-PUNK EBB & THE ROCK DIASPORA

Guitar rock lost its chart dominance but thrived in pockets: arena-folk-rock crossovers, a lingering emo/pop-punk scene, and heavier alternative. Rock became one niche among many rather than the center.

## SOUNDCLOUD RAP & THE GENRE-BLUR ENDGAME

Late in the decade, a raw, emo-influenced, melodically loose generation emerged from SoundCloud, and genre fully collapsed. The ultimate symbol: Lil Nas X's 'Old Town Road,' a country-trap song that became the longest-running #1 ever and proved categories no longer meant anything.

## COUNTRY: BRO-COUNTRY & THE POP FUSION

Country doubled down on pop and hip-hop crossover -- 'bro-country' with rap cadences and party themes dominated the radio, while pop-country blurred formats completely and set up the genre wars over what 'counts' as country.
