# The Story of Indian & South Asian Music — Master Guide

*A chronological, listenable survey of the music of India and the wider South Asia — Pakistan,
Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Nepal, and the diaspora — across seven installments, from the Vedas to the
streaming age.*

South Asian music is one of humanity's deepest and most prolific musical worlds: two great classical
systems, a devotional tradition older than most religions' scriptures, thousands of living folk forms,
the largest film-song culture on Earth, and a diaspora that reshaped global pop. This guide stitches the
seven installments into one arc: from the raga and the Samaveda, through folk and devotion, to the
Bollywood juggernaut and a global streaming present.

The full deduplicated listening list is `MASTER_SOUTH_ASIA_FULL_SURVEY_IMPORT.txt` — **318 tracks**,
zero cross-installment duplicates. Each installment also has its own `.md` and `_IMPORT.txt`.

## The seven installments

**1 — The Classical Roots: Raga, Tala & the Two Great Systems.**
`south_asia_music_1_CLASSICAL_ROOTS_RAGA_TALA` (48). The Vedic/Samaveda deep roots; **raga** (melodic
framework) and **tala** (rhythmic cycle); the guru-shishya oral tradition and gharanas; the
**Hindustani/Carnatic split**; the instruments; and the devotional bhakti heart.

**2 — The Classical Masters (Hindustani & Carnatic, c. 1900-present).**
`south_asia_music_2_CLASSICAL_MASTERS` (45). The concert age; the sitar/sarod giants (Ravi Shankar, Ali
Akbar Khan); the vocal masters (Bhimsen Joshi, Kesarbai Kerkar on the Voyager record); tabla (Zakir
Hussain); the Carnatic voice (**M.S. Subbulakshmi**); and the raga going global.

**3 — Folk & Regional Traditions: the Village India.**
`south_asia_music_3_FOLK_REGIONAL_TRADITIONS` (50). Bengal's bauls & Tagore; Rajasthan's desert
Manganiar; Punjab's bhangra; Maharashtra's lavani; Gujarat's garba; the Tamil south; Assam & the
Northeast; and the Adivasi/tribal substratum.

**4 — Devotional Traditions: Bhajan, Qawwali & the Sacred Voice.**
`south_asia_music_4_DEVOTIONAL_BHAJAN_QAWWALI` (43). The bhakti poet-saints; Hindu bhajan/kirtan; the
Sufi shrine and **qawwali** (Amir Khusrau, **Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan**, the Sabri Brothers, Abida
Parveen); Sikh Gurbani; and the global kirtan.

**5 — The Film-Song Juggernaut: Bollywood's Golden Age (c. 1931-1970).**
`south_asia_music_5_BOLLYWOOD_GOLDEN_AGE` (43). The birth of playback; the great music directors
(Naushad, S.D. Burman); and the immortal voices — **Lata Mangeshkar**, **Mohammed Rafi**, Kishore
Kumar, Mukesh — that made the film song India's true pop.

**6 — Modern Filmi & the Indipop Boom (c. 1970-2010).**
`south_asia_music_6_MODERN_FILMI_INDIPOP` (45). R.D. Burman's revolution; the disco era; the ghazal
revival (Jagjit Singh, Mehdi Hassan); Ilaiyaraaja; **A.R. Rahman**'s transformation (the Oscar-winning
"Jai Ho"); and the 1990s Indipop boom.

**7 — Modern, Global & the Wider South Asia (c. 2010-present).**
`south_asia_music_7_MODERN_GLOBAL_WIDER_SOUTH_ASIA` (44). Arijit Singh & streaming-era filmi; the global
**Punjabi-pop** juggernaut; Indian indie & gully rap; **Pakistan** (Coke Studio, "Pasoori");
**Bangladesh**, **Sri Lanka** (Yohani), **Nepal**; and the global **diaspora** (Panjabi MC, M.I.A.).

## Through-lines

- **The raga as deep grammar.** Raga and tala underlie everything — classical, devotional, folk, and
  even the film song — a shared musical language across a billion-plus people and many faiths.
- **Devotion and cinema, the twin engines.** South Asian popular music has two great motors: the sacred
  (bhajan, kirtan, qawwali, shabad) and the screen (the film song, which never needed a separate pop
  industry because the movies WERE the pop industry).
- **One music, many nations and faiths.** Hindu, Muslim, Sikh, and Christian devotion; Indian, Pakistani,
  Bangladeshi, Sri Lankan, and Nepali scenes; and a shared Hindustani/qawwali/ghazal heritage that
  crosses the borders drawn in 1947 — the survey keeps the whole region in view, flagging cross-border
  and diaspora artists.
- **The village beneath the canopy.** Beneath the classical and film worlds lies the vast folk reservoir
  — the true bedrock that every "higher" form draws from.
- **The global diaspora.** From Ravi Shankar and the Beatles to bhangra, Nusrat's Real World records,
  Coke Studio, M.I.A., and the Punjabi-pop wave, South Asian sound is now woven through global pop —
  cross-linking into the US, UK, and Western surveys.

## Notes on method

Named entries cite a canonical work/song and performer; **bare titles** mark ragas, talas, forms,
traditions, or artists best used as search seeds (the classical/folk/devotional installments lean on
these; the film installments are named-song-heavy). Deduplication is by **title only**, so every title
across the seven installments is unique. Scope: the Horn/Central Asia and East/Southeast Asia are
separate surveys.
