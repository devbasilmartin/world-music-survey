# The Story of European Music — Installment 1: Medieval & Renaissance (c. 800-1600)

Europe's survey starts a thousand years before America's, because Europe has the deepest
continuous tradition of NOTATED music on earth -- and it braids with living folk traditions
the whole way. The two throughlines to follow from the very start: (1) the invention and
perfection of POLYPHONY (many independent voices at once) -- from chant to Perotin to Josquin
to Palestrina, the defining achievement of Western music; and (2) the parallel SECULAR/FOLK
stream -- troubadours, madrigals, dances -- feeding and feeding off the sacred tradition.
Named performers are respected early-music recordings; (Traditional) = search by title.

## SACRED FOUNDATION: PLAINCHANT (c. 800-1100)

Western notated music begins in the church. Gregorian chant -- monophonic, unmetered, Latin liturgical song -- is the bedrock the entire art-music tradition grows from, and the first music in Europe systematically written down. Everything downstream (harmony, counterpoint, the staff itself) is invented to serve and extend it.

- Puer natus est nobis (Introit) — Gregorian Chant
- Kyrie eleison (Mass IX) — Gregorian Chant
- Veni Creator Spiritus — Gregorian Chant
- Dies Irae — Gregorian Chant
- Victimae Paschali Laudes — Gregorian Chant
- Salve Regina — Gregorian Chant

## THE FIRST NAMED COMPOSER & EARLY POLYPHONY (1100-1200)

Two revolutions. Hildegard von Bingen -- abbess, mystic, and the first composer in history we know by name -- writes soaring, personal sacred song. And at Notre-Dame in Paris, Leonin and Perotin stack voices into 'organum,' the birth of Western polyphony (multiple independent melodic lines at once), the single most consequential idea in European music.

- O virtus Sapientiae — Hildegard von Bingen
- Columba aspexit — Hildegard von Bingen
- O Euchari — Hildegard von Bingen
- Viderunt omnes — Perotin
- Sederunt principes — Perotin

## SECULAR SONG: TROUBADOURS, TROUVERES & MINNESANG (1100-1300)

Alongside the church, a courtly secular song tradition flowers -- troubadours in Occitan southern France, trouveres in the north, Minnesanger in the German lands, and the cantigas of Iberia. Songs of courtly love, crusade, and dance, in the vernacular. This is Europe's deep folk-art root, and the ancestor of every love song since.

- Kalenda Maya — Raimbaut de Vaqueiras
- Can vei la lauzeta mover — Bernart de Ventadorn
- A chantar m'er de so qu'ieu non volria — Comtessa de Dia
- Cantiga 100: Santa Maria, Strela do dia — Alfonso X el Sabio
- Cantiga 166: Como poden per sas culpas — Alfonso X el Sabio
- Palastinalied — Walther von der Vogelweide
- Douce dame jolie — Guillaume de Machaut

## ARS NOVA: RHYTHM & THE 14th-CENTURY LEAP (1300-1400)

Music grows rhythmically and structurally complex. Guillaume de Machaut -- poet-composer -- writes the first complete polyphonic mass setting by one author and refines the secular chanson into high art. Italy's Trecento (Landini) adds its own sweetness. Notation now captures subtlety it never could before.

- Messe de Nostre Dame: Kyrie — Guillaume de Machaut
- Rose, lis, printemps, verdure — Guillaume de Machaut
- Ma fin est mon commencement — Guillaume de Machaut
- Non avra ma' pieta — Francesco Landini
- Ecco la primavera — Francesco Landini

## EARLY RENAISSANCE: THE FRANCO-FLEMISH MASTERS (1400-1500)

The center of gravity moves to the Low Countries. Dufay bridges medieval and Renaissance; Josquin des Prez becomes the first composer with a 'genius' reputation across Europe, perfecting seamless imitative counterpoint. Sacred polyphony reaches a new expressive richness that spreads through every European court.

- Nuper rosarum flores — Guillaume Dufay
- Se la face ay pale — Guillaume Dufay
- Missa Prolationum: Kyrie — Johannes Ockeghem
- Ave Maria... virgo serena — Josquin des Prez
- Missa Pange Lingua: Kyrie — Josquin des Prez
- Mille regretz — Josquin des Prez

## HIGH RENAISSANCE: THE GOLDEN AGE OF POLYPHONY (1500-1600)

Choral polyphony reaches its summit. Palestrina in Rome perfects a serene, balanced sacred style; Lassus roams every genre and language; Byrd and Tallis serve the turbulent English church; Victoria brings Spanish mystical intensity. This is the sound of the Counter-Reformation cathedral -- the peak of the a cappella tradition.

- Missa Papae Marcelli: Kyrie — Giovanni Pierluigi da Palestrina
- Sicut cervus — Giovanni Pierluigi da Palestrina
- O magnum mysterium — Tomas Luis de Victoria
- Spem in alium — Thomas Tallis
- Ave verum corpus — William Byrd
- Tristis est anima mea — Orlando di Lasso

## THE RENAISSANCE SECULAR WORLD: MADRIGALS & DANCES (1500-1600)

Outside the church, a rich secular repertoire: the Italian madrigal (word-painting emotion in polyphony), the English madrigal school, lute songs, and instrumental dance music. Court and household music-making becomes a mark of the cultivated Renaissance person.

- Il bianco e dolce cigno — Jacques Arcadelt
- Moro, lasso, al mio duolo — Carlo Gesualdo
- As Vesta Was from Latmos Hill Descending — Thomas Weelkes
- Now Is the Month of Maying — Thomas Morley
- Flow My Tears — John Dowland
- Come Again, Sweet Love Doth Now Invite — John Dowland

## LIVING FOLK ROOTS (traditional, timeless)

Running underneath all the notated music, the oral folk traditions -- dance tunes, ballads, seasonal and work songs -- that were never written down at the time but survive as living tradition. These are modern performances of very old material; treat them as the folk bedrock that composers drew on for centuries. Search by title for a version you like.
