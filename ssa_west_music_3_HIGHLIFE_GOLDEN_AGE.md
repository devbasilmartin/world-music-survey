# The Story of West African Music — Installment 3: Highlife's Golden Age (c. 1950-1969)

This is West Africa's first great popular flowering -- and it arrives with independence. HIGHLIFE, seeded in Installment 2 from palm-wine guitar and colonial dance bands, becomes in the 1950s the continent's most sophisticated pop: swinging horn sections, Cuban-tinged rhythm, and lyrics in Twi, Yoruba, Igbo, and pidgin. Its king is E.T. MENSAH, whose Tempos toured all West Africa; when Ghana becomes the first sub-Saharan colony to win independence in 1957, highlife is the sound of the party, and Kwame Nkrumah's pan-African optimism rides its beat. Two streams run in parallel: the elite DANCE-BAND highlife of the cities and the grassroots GUITAR-BAND highlife of the traveling CONCERT PARTY theatres. In Nigeria the music takes fire too -- Bobby Benson and Victor Olaiya in Lagos, and a brilliant Igbo highlife in the east that the Biafran war (1967-70) will tragically cut down. Cross-links are everywhere: Louis Armstrong toured the Gold Coast in 1956 and jammed with Mensah (US survey); imported Cuban 'GV' son records give highlife its 'Latin tinge' (Latin survey); and Congolese rumba is crossing westward (the Central Africa survey to come). Content note: this is music of decolonization and, in the east, of a war -- kept with its context. Region: all named artists are Ghanaian or Nigerian.

## E.T. MENSAH & THE TEMPOS: THE KING OF HIGHLIFE (Ghana, c. 1948-1965)

E.T. Mensah and his Tempos perfected dance-band highlife: a tight horn-led swing built for the ballroom, sung in pidgin and Twi, that made him a star from Accra to Lagos and 'the King of Highlife'. His songs mixed romance, social comment, and the euphoria of the independence years. Cross-link: Louis Armstrong sat in with the Tempos on his 1956 Gold Coast tour.

- All For You — E.T. Mensah
- Ghana Freedom — E.T. Mensah
- Day By Day — E.T. Mensah
- 205 — E.T. Mensah
- School Girl — E.T. Mensah
- Munsuro — E.T. Mensah

## GUITAR-BAND HIGHLIFE & THE CONCERT PARTY (Ghana, c. 1950-1969)

Below the ballroom ran the people's highlife: guitar bands that toured the villages with the CONCERT PARTY, a form of comic musical theatre in Akan. E.K. Nyame was its towering figure, Kwaa Mensah carried the palm-wine guitar line, and in 1967 Nana Ampadu's African Brothers cut 'Ebi Te Yie' ('Some Are Well-Seated'), a proverb-song so pointed the government took notice -- highlife as veiled political speech.

## NIGERIA: THE LAGOS HIGHLIFE DANCE BANDS (c. 1950-1966)

Highlife swept into cosmopolitan Lagos, where Bobby Benson's band was a finishing school for a generation and his 'Taxi Driver' became an anthem, while Victor Olaiya -- 'the Evil Genius of Highlife' -- ruled the bandstand and played the 1960 independence ball. Roy Chicago and E.C. Arinze kept the horn-driven dance floors full.

## EASTERN NIGERIA: IGBO HIGHLIFE & THE BIAFRA SHADOW (c. 1955-1969)

The east grew a gorgeous, guitar-forward Igbo highlife. Cardinal Rex Lawson (a Kalabari from the Delta) was its biggest star; Celestine Ukwu brought a philosophical calm; and Osita Osadebe was beginning the career that would later crown him. Content note: this vibrant scene was shattered by the Biafran war (1967-70) -- Rex Lawson died young in 1971, and Ukwu in 1977; the music here is an elegy as much as a party.

## THE LATIN TINGE & THE CONGO CONNECTION (thematic)

Highlife was a Black-Atlantic sponge. Its horn charts drew on American swing; imported Cuban 'GV' (Gramophone Victor) son 78s gave it a rumba-clave 'Latin tinge'; calypso traded licks with it across the coast; and from the other side of the continent, Congolese rumba-soukous was beginning its westward march. Cross-links: to the Latin America survey (son/clave), to the coming Central Africa survey (Congo rumba), and to the US (swing horns).

## THE DANCE-BAND CIRCUIT & THE HIGHLIFE SONGBOOK (c. 1955-1969)

A whole touring economy grew up around highlife: Ghana's Ramblers, Broadway, and Uhuru dance bands, the Ghana-Nigeria club circuit, and a first generation of women stepping to the microphone. This is the mature ecosystem that the juju and Afrobeat revolutions of the coming installments will grow out of -- and rebel against.
