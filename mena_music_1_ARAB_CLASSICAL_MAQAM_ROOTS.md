# The Story of Middle Eastern & North African Music — Installment 1: Arab Classical & Maqam Roots

The music of the Arab world rests on one of humanity's most sophisticated systems: the MAQAM, a framework of modes built not on twelve equal semitones but on subtle microtones and quarter-tones, each maqam a distinct emotional world with its own melodic path (sayr). Paired with the IQA'AT (rhythmic modes) and performed by the intimate TAKHT chamber ensemble -- oud, qanun, ney, kamanja, riqq -- it aims at TARAB, a shared ecstatic rapture between musician and listener. Its forms range from the improvised taqsim and mawwal to the strophic dawr, the sung-poetry qasida, and the exquisite MUWASHSHAH inherited from Andalusian Spain (where the legendary Ziryab had transformed music centuries before). The 19th-century NAHDA (Arab renaissance) and pioneers like Sayyid Darwish carried this deep classical lineage -- centered on Egypt and Aleppo -- into the modern age. This installment lays the theoretical and classical foundation before the Egyptian golden age, and the Persian, Turkish, and Maghrebi threads, to come. Cross-links: the shared maqam/makam/dastgah family with Turkey and Iran (installments ahead); al-Andalus feeding both flamenco (Europe survey) and the Maghreb (#5); the oud as ancestor of the European lute. Content note: Quranic recitation and Sufi chant are treated respectfully as vocal arts that shaped the tradition. Names transliterated. Region: the Arab world (Egypt, the Levant/Aleppo, and the wider Mashriq).

## THE MAQAM: THE SOUL OF ARAB MUSIC

A maqam is far more than a scale: it is a mode with a characteristic set of notes (including microtones and quarter-tones alien to the Western piano), a built-in melodic path, and a distinct emotional color. Rast, Bayati, Hijaz, Saba, and Nahawand each evoke a specific mood, and mastery means knowing how to travel through them.

## THE IQA'AT: THE RHYTHMIC MODES

Arab rhythm is organized into iqa'at -- cycles of low 'dum' and sharp 'tak' strokes of different lengths. From the ubiquitous Maqsum and Baladi to the long, stately Samai Thaqil and the heavy Masmudi, these patterns give each piece its gait, played on the riqq, tabla (darbuka), and daff.

## THE TAKHT: THE CLASSICAL ENSEMBLE

Classical Arab music is chamber music: the TAKHT, a small ensemble of a handful of master instrumentalists and a lead singer (mutrib), often with a small backing chorus. Its intimate, conversational interplay -- responsive to the singer and the audience alike -- is the natural setting for tarab.

## THE INSTRUMENTS

The Arab instrumentarium is ancient and beautiful. The fretless OUD -- the 'king of instruments' and ancestor of the European lute -- leads; the plucked-zither QANUN shimmers with its 78 strings and quarter-tone levers; the reed flute NEY breathes a Sufi sound; the kamanja (violin) sings; and the buzuq and riqq complete the ensemble.

## THE FORMS: TAQSIM, MAWWAL, DAWR & QASIDA

Arab classical music balances composed and improvised. The TAQSIM is a soloist's modal improvisation; the layali and MAWWAL are its vocal counterparts; the DAWR is an Egyptian strophic song with call-and-response; the QASIDA sets classical poetry; and a full evening unfolds as a WASLA, a suite in a single maqam.

## THE MUWASHSHAH & THE ANDALUSIAN LEGACY

The MUWASHSHAH -- an elegant strophic song form -- is the great inheritance of Muslim Spain, where in the 9th century the legendary ZIRYAB brought the music of Baghdad to Cordoba, added a fifth string to the oud, and founded a conservatory. 'Lamma Bada Yatathanna' is its most beloved example. After 1492 the tradition was preserved in the Maghreb. Cross-link: al-Andalus -> flamenco & the Maghreb (#5).

## TARAB & THE ART OF ECSTASY

The goal of all this is TARAB -- a state of ecstatic enchantment shared between performer and listener. The singer, in a state of modal immersion (saltana), improvises and repeats phrases; the audience calls back ('Allah!', 'Ya Salam!') and urges the music higher. It is among the most participatory listening cultures on Earth.

## THE NAHDA & THE EARLY MASTERS (c. 1850-1920)

The 19th-century NAHDA (Arab renaissance) revitalized the classical arts. In Egypt, Abdo al-Hamuli and the singer Almaz refined the dawr; Aleppo remained the northern citadel of the muwashshah and the qudud halabiyya; and Sayyid Darwish -- the father of modern Arab music -- fused classical craft with popular and national feeling ('Zuruni'), opening the door to the modern age.
