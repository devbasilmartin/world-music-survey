# The Story of Caribbean Music — Master Guide

*A chronological, listenable survey of the music of the Caribbean — Cuba, Jamaica, Trinidad, Haiti, the
Dominican Republic, Puerto Rico, the French Antilles, and beyond — from the Afro-Cuban clave and the son
to reggae, calypso, dancehall, and the global reggaeton present, across four installments.*

No sea of its size has given the world more rhythm. Out of the meeting of Africa and Europe in the
plantations and ports of the Caribbean came an astonishing succession of musics — the Afro-Cuban clave
and son, Jamaican reggae and dub, Trinidadian calypso and the steelpan, Haitian kompa, Dominican
merengue, Puerto Rican bomba, French-Antillean zouk, and finally dancehall and reggaeton, today the
biggest sound on Earth. This guide stitches the four installments into one arc, from the clave to the
dembow.

The full deduplicated listening list is `MASTER_CARIB_FULL_SURVEY_IMPORT.txt` — **172 tracks**, zero
cross-installment duplicates. Each installment also has its own `.md` and `_IMPORT.txt`.

## The four installments

**1 — Cuban Roots & the Afro-Caribbean Foundation.** `carib_music_1_CUBAN_AFRO_ROOTS` (44). The **clave**
and the African-Spanish fusion; the **son** (Trio Matamoros, the Buena Vista Social Club revival);
**rumba** (guaguanco/yambu/columbia); the Afro-Cuban sacred (**bata** drums, **Santeria**); the salon
(**danzon**, charanga, **bolero**); the **mambo** & **cha-cha-cha** (Perez Prado, Beny More); and
Afro-Cuban jazz (Chano Pozo, Machito).

**2 — Jamaica: Ska, Rocksteady, Reggae & Dub.** `carib_music_2_JAMAICA_SKA_REGGAE_DUB` (42). **Mento** and
**Rastafari**/nyabinghi; the **ska** boom (the Skatalites) and the sound system; **rocksteady** (Alton
Ellis); **roots reggae** and **Bob Marley** (Peter Tosh, Burning Spear, Toots, Jimmy Cliff); **dub** and
the studio as instrument (King Tubby, Lee "Scratch" Perry, Augustus Pablo); and **toasting** (U-Roy), the
root of rap.

**3 — Calypso, Soca, Steelpan & the Wider Isles.** `carib_music_3_CALYPSO_SOCA_WIDER_ISLES` (43). Trinidad
**calypso** & Carnival (Mighty Sparrow, Lord Kitchener), **soca** (Lord Shorty, Machel Montano), and the
**steelpan**; **Haiti** (kompa, rara, mizik rasin); the **Dominican Republic** (merengue, bachata);
**Puerto Rico** (bomba & plena); and the **French Antilles** (zouk/Kassav', biguine, gwoka).

**4 — Dancehall, Reggaeton & the Modern Caribbean (Finale).** `carib_music_4_DANCEHALL_REGGAETON_MODERN`
(43). The digital turn and **dancehall** (Sleng Teng, Yellowman, Shabba Ranks, Sean Paul, Vybz Kartel);
the roots and explosion of **reggaeton** (El General, Daddy Yankee's "Gasolina", the global J Balvin/**Bad
Bunny** era); **Rihanna** and the Caribbean-global superstar; and soca's global fete.

## Through-lines

- **The African root and the drum.** From the bata and rumba of Cuba to nyabinghi in Jamaica, Vodou rara
  in Haiti, and bomba in Puerto Rico, the deep African inheritance — carried by the enslaved and kept in
  sacred and secular drumming — is the bedrock of all Caribbean music.
- **The clave, the offbeat, and the riddim.** The Caribbean's gift to the world is rhythm: the Cuban
  clave, reggae's offbeat skank and one-drop, and the dembow of reggaeton are all versions of one genius
  for the groove.
- **The studio and the DJ.** Jamaica reinvented recorded music — dub's remix science, the riddim system,
  and the toasting DJ — seeding hip-hop, remix culture, and reggaeton across the globe.
- **Carnival and the fete.** From Trinidad's calypso and soca to the diaspora Carnivals of Notting Hill
  and Brooklyn, the culture of Carnival and the party is a Caribbean throughline.
- **A small sea, a global reach.** Reggae, dub, dancehall, and reggaeton — and stars from Bob Marley to
  Rihanna and Bad Bunny — made this small sea one of the most influential musical regions on Earth, its
  sound now at the center of global pop.

## Notes on method & scope

Named entries cite a canonical song and performer; **bare titles** mark genres, rhythms, instruments, or
traditions best used as search seeds (the roots-heavy Cuban and wider-isles installments lean on these;
the Jamaica and modern installments are named-heavy). All entries are transliterated, ASCII-only.
Deduplication is by **title only**, so every title within the region is unique. Sensitive material — the
Afro-Cuban and Haitian Vodou-rooted sacred traditions, dancehall's slackness and anti-gay-lyric
controversies, reggaeton's censorship battles and Vybz Kartel's legal case — is presented factually,
evenhandedly, and with care, as music. Scope note: **salsa** and the broader **reggaeton** story also
live in the Latin America survey; here they are cross-linked, with the Caribbean/Cuban/Puerto-Rican-origin
tracks kept in this survey rather than re-explaining the New York and pan-Latin narratives.
