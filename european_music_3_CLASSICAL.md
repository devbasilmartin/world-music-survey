# The Story of European Music — Installment 3: The Classical Era (c. 1750-1820)

The Classical era prized CLARITY, BALANCE, and singable melody after the Baroque's dense
counterpoint. Throughlines: (1) HAYDN builds the architecture -- symphony, string quartet, and
sonata form, the dramatic structure behind nearly all instrumental music to come; (2) MOZART
perfects and humanizes it with miraculous fluency across every genre; and (3) BEETHOVEN blows
it all open -- longer, more personal, heroic -- turning music into individual statement and
dragging it single-handedly toward Romanticism. Folk traditions run alongside and cross-feed.

## THE STYLE SHIFT: FROM BAROQUE COMPLEXITY TO CLASSICAL CLARITY

After Bach, taste turned against dense counterpoint toward elegance, balance, and clear melody-plus-accompaniment. The 'galant' and early Classical style prized naturalness and wit -- shorter phrases, singable tunes, clean forms. Bach's own sons helped lead the shift away from their father's world.

- Symphony in E-flat major, Wq. 179 — Carl Philipp Emanuel Bach
- Keyboard Sonata in A major (La) — Domenico Scarlatti
- Keyboard Sonata in D minor, K. 141 — Domenico Scarlatti
- Stabat Mater: Stabat Mater dolorosa — Giovanni Battista Pergolesi
- Che faro senza Euridice — Christoph Willibald Gluck

## HAYDN: THE ARCHITECT OF CLASSICAL FORM (1732-1809)

Joseph Haydn essentially invented the two defining Classical genres -- the symphony and the string quartet -- and perfected sonata form, the dramatic key-based structure underpinning nearly all instrumental music for the next century. Witty, inventive, and prolific, he set the template Mozart and Beethoven built on.

- Symphony No. 94 (Surprise): Andante — Joseph Haydn
- Symphony No. 104 (London): Finale — Joseph Haydn
- String Quartet Op. 76 No. 3 (Emperor): Poco adagio — Joseph Haydn
- The Creation: The Heavens Are Telling — Joseph Haydn
- Trumpet Concerto in E-flat major: Finale — Joseph Haydn
- Cello Concerto No. 1 in C major: Moderato — Joseph Haydn

## MOZART: THE PERFECTION OF THE STYLE (1756-1791)

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart absorbed every form and genre and wrote in all of them with a fluency and expressive depth that still seems miraculous -- operas of psychological insight, luminous concertos, and symphonies of perfect proportion. If Haydn built the house, Mozart made it sing.

- Eine kleine Nachtmusik: Allegro — Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart
- Symphony No. 40 in G minor: Molto allegro — Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart
- Symphony No. 41 (Jupiter): Finale — Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart
- Piano Concerto No. 21: Andante — Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart
- The Marriage of Figaro: Overture — Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart
- Don Giovanni: Overture — Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart
- The Magic Flute: Der Holle Rache (Queen of the Night) — Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart
- Requiem: Lacrimosa — Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart
- Clarinet Concerto in A major: Adagio — Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart
- Rondo alla Turca (Piano Sonata No. 11) — Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart

## BEETHOVEN: THE REVOLUTIONARY BRIDGE (1770-1827)

Ludwig van Beethoven took the Classical forms Haydn and Mozart perfected and blew them open -- longer, louder, more personal and heroic, driven by struggle and triumph. Composing through deafness, he transformed music from courtly entertainment into individual artistic statement, and single-handedly dragged music into the Romantic era.

- Piano Sonata No. 14 (Moonlight): Adagio sostenuto — Ludwig van Beethoven
- Fur Elise — Ludwig van Beethoven
- Piano Sonata No. 8 (Pathetique): Adagio cantabile — Ludwig van Beethoven
- Piano Concerto No. 5 (Emperor): Adagio — Ludwig van Beethoven
- Violin Concerto in D major: Larghetto — Ludwig van Beethoven
- String Quartet No. 14 in C-sharp minor — Ludwig van Beethoven
