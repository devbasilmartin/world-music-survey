# The Story of European Music — Installment 7: The Postwar Avant-Garde & Late-20th-C Art Music (c. 1945-2000)

The concert-hall stream after WWII. This is the specialized, sometimes difficult end of the
survey -- flagged honestly. Throughlines: (1) the avant-garde pushes to the EXTREME -- total
serialism (Boulez, Stockhausen) organizing every note by rule; (2) Europe INVENTS electronic
music (Schaeffer's musique concrete, Cologne, the BBC Workshop) -- the ancestor of all dance
music; (3) texture/sound-mass composition (Ligeti, Penderecki); then (4) a swing back to
beauty -- 'holy minimalism' (Part, Gorecki), some of the most-loved music of the era. The
European FILM SCORE (Morricone, Rota) bridges art music and pop. Difficulty is flagged per section.

## MESSIAEN & THE POSTWAR ROOTS (c. 1945-1960)

Olivier Messiaen -- devout Catholic, obsessed with birdsong, rhythm, and color -- becomes the towering teacher whose Paris class trains the entire next generation. His music bridges late Romanticism and radical modernism, and is far more listenable than what his students went on to do.

- Quartet for the End of Time: Louange a l'Eternite de Jesus — Olivier Messiaen
- Turangalila-Symphonie: Joie du sang des etoiles — Olivier Messiaen

## TOTAL SERIALISM & THE DARMSTADT SCHOOL (c. 1950-1965) [challenging]

The postwar generation, wanting a total break from a culture that had produced fascism, pushed Schoenberg's 12-tone method to the extreme -- 'total serialism,' where every parameter (pitch, rhythm, dynamics) is governed by rule. Boulez and Stockhausen led this uncompromising, cerebral avant-garde. Difficult, radical, historically pivotal -- and, fair warning, hard listening.

- Le Marteau sans maitre — Pierre Boulez
- Klavierstuck IX — Karlheinz Stockhausen

## THE INVENTION OF ELECTRONIC MUSIC (c. 1948-1970)

One of Europe's most consequential 20th-century contributions: sound recording and electronics as a compositional medium. In Paris, Pierre Schaeffer invents musique concrete (music built from recorded sound); in Cologne, Stockhausen builds purely electronic tones; in London, the BBC Radiophonic Workshop makes early electronic pop. This is the direct ancestor of ALL electronic and dance music to come.

- Etude aux chemins de fer — Pierre Schaeffer
- Gesang der Junglinge — Karlheinz Stockhausen
- Kontakte — Karlheinz Stockhausen
- Doctor Who Theme (1963) — Delia Derbyshire and Ron Grainer

## SONORISM, TEXTURE & CHANCE (c. 1960-1975) [challenging]

A wave of composers abandoned melody and harmony for pure sound-mass, cluster, and texture. Ligeti's shimmering micropolyphony (which Kubrick made famous in '2001'), Penderecki's screaming string clusters, Xenakis's mathematically-built architecture, Lutoslawski's controlled chance. Radical -- but Ligeti especially can be hauntingly beautiful.

- Atmospheres — Gyorgy Ligeti
- Lux Aeterna — Gyorgy Ligeti
- Threnody to the Victims of Hiroshima — Krzysztof Penderecki
- Metastaseis — Iannis Xenakis
- Concerto for Orchestra — Witold Lutoslawski

## HOLY MINIMALISM & THE SPIRITUAL TURN (c. 1975-2000) [very accessible]

A reaction against avant-garde severity: a return to consonance, stillness, slowness, and spirituality. Estonia's Arvo Part (his hushed 'tintinnabuli' style), Poland's Gorecki, and Britain's Tavener wrote sacred, meditative music that became some of the most beloved and widely-heard concert music of the late century. Start here if the challenging sections aren't for you.

- Spiegel im Spiegel — Arvo Part
- Fur Alina — Arvo Part
- Fratres — Arvo Part
- Symphony No. 3 (Symphony of Sorrowful Songs): Lento — Henryk Gorecki
- The Lamb — John Tavener
- Song for Athene — John Tavener

## THE EUROPEAN FILM SCORE (c. 1955-1990)

European composers redefined film music, and this became some of the most widely-heard 'classical-adjacent' music in the world -- the bridge between art music and pop. Italy's Ennio Morricone (the spaghetti westerns and far beyond) and Nino Rota (Fellini, and 'The Godfather'), France's Michel Legrand.

- The Good, the Bad and the Ugly: Main Theme — Ennio Morricone
- Once Upon a Time in the West — Ennio Morricone
- Cinema Paradiso: Love Theme — Ennio Morricone
- The Godfather: Speak Softly Love (Love Theme) — Nino Rota
- La Strada — Nino Rota
- The Umbrellas of Cherbourg: I Will Wait for You — Michel Legrand

## LIVING FOLK & REGIONAL TRADITIONS ALONGSIDE

The postwar folk revivals and enduring regional traditions that keep feeding European popular music -- Iberian flamenco and fado, Greek and Balkan song, and the deep well of national folk. A mix of named masters and living tradition.
