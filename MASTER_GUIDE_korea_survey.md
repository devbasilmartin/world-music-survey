# The Story of Korean Music — Master Guide

*A chronological, listenable survey of Korean music — from pansori and the gayageum to trot, the
developmental decades, and a world-conquering K-pop — across five installments.*

Korean music turns deep feeling into sound. Its traditional art is built on *han* (aching sorrow and
longing) and *heung* (irrepressible joy); its modern story is one of survival — through occupation,
war, and dictatorship — and then of an astonishing global triumph. This guide stitches the five
installments into one arc, from the shaman's drum and the pansori singer's raw voice to BTS and
BLACKPINK.

The full deduplicated listening list is `MASTER_KOREA_FULL_SURVEY_IMPORT.txt` — **205 tracks**, zero
cross-installment duplicates. Each installment also has its own `.md` and `_IMPORT.txt`.

## The five installments

**1 — Classical & Folk Roots (gugak).** `korea_music_1_CLASSICAL_FOLK_ROOTS_GUGAK` (46). The Korean
aesthetic (*han*/*heung*/*meot*); court and ritual music (aak, jongmyo jeryeak, "Sujecheon"); **pansori**
epic story-singing; **sanjo**; the **gayageum** and **geomungo**; the daegeum/piri/haegeum; **pungmul**
and samulnori percussion; shamanic **gut**; and folk minyo ("Arirang").

**2 — Occupation, Trot & Early Pop (c. 1900-1960).** `korea_music_2_OCCUPATION_TROT_EARLY_POP` (35).
Colonial rule and the suppression of gugak; changga; the birth of **trot** (Lee Nan-young's "Tears of
Mokpo"); liberation and division; the **Korean War**'s wrenching songs ("Farewell at Busan Station");
and the US bases / AFKN American invasion.

**3 — The Developmental Decades: Folk, Rock, Trot & the Ballad (c. 1960-1987).**
`korea_music_3_FOLK_ROCK_TROT_BALLAD` (37). The Eighth-Army stars; **Shin Joong-hyun** and the birth of
Korean rock; the **folk** counterculture (Kim Min-gi's "Morning Dew"); trot's golden age (Lee Mi-ja);
**Cho Yong-pil** the superstar; and music under dictatorship and the 1975 crackdown.

**4 — The Birth of K-pop & the Hallyu Wave (c. 1992-2009).** `korea_music_4_KPOP_BIRTH_HALLYU` (45).
**Seo Taiji and Boys**' 1992 revolution; the **agency idol-factory** system; the first idols (H.O.T.,
S.E.S.); the 90s ballad/R&B; **BoA and TVXQ** conquering Japan; the early **Hallyu**; and the 2nd-gen
idols (Big Bang, Girls' Generation, Wonder Girls, 2NE1).

**5 — Modern & Global K-pop (c. 2010-present).** `korea_music_5_MODERN_GLOBAL_KPOP` (42). Psy's
**"Gangnam Style"**; **BTS** and the global explosion; **BLACKPINK**; the 3rd-gen constellation (EXO,
TWICE, Red Velvet); the 4th generation (Stray Kids, aespa, **NewJeans**); **K-hip-hop** (Zico, Jay
Park); K-indie and K-R&B (**IU**); and the soft-power juggernaut.

## Through-lines

- **Han and heung.** The two emotional poles of pansori and gugak — deep sorrow and irrepressible joy —
  run straight through trot's tearful ballads and even the euphoric drive of K-pop.
- **Music and the state.** Korean music was repeatedly shaped by power: colonial suppression, wartime
  loss, decades of censorship and the 1975 crackdown, and finally K-pop as deliberate national soft
  power. (Sensitive material is kept factual and brief.)
- **Absorbing foreign sound, making it Korean.** Trot from enka and foxtrot, rock and folk from the US
  bases, and K-pop from American hip-hop, R&B, and Japanese idol pop — each time refined into something
  distinctly Korean and, ultimately, re-exported.
- **From survival to soft power.** The arc runs from a colonized, war-torn, censored music to the
  planet's most successful pop export — Hallyu — carrying Korean sound and stardom worldwide.
- **The East Asian family.** Gugak is pentatonic and shares roots with China and Japan, yet its loose
  triple-meter swing and emotional rawness make it unmistakably its own.

## Notes on method & scope

Named entries cite a canonical song and performer; **bare titles** mark forms, instruments, or artists
best used as search seeds (the roots installments lean on these; the pop installments are named-heavy).
All entries are romanized, ASCII-only. Deduplication is by **title only**, so every title within the
sub-region is unique. Scope: Korea (primarily the South after 1945); China and Japan are separate East
Asia surveys. **With Korea, all of East Asia (China, Japan, Korea) is complete.**
