# The Story of Chinese Music — Master Guide

*A chronological, listenable survey of Chinese music — from the guqin and the pentatonic ideal to
shidaiqu, the Mao era, the gangtai golden age, and the streaming present — across five installments.*

Chinese music is one of the world's oldest continuous traditions and, today, one of its largest
markets. This guide stitches the five installments into one arc: a 3,000-year story of a pentatonic,
philosophically weighted art that survived dynastic collapse, revolution, a near-total silencing, and
a spectacular pop rebirth. Through every upheaval, a recognizable Chinese musical soul persists.

The full deduplicated listening list is `MASTER_CHINA_FULL_SURVEY_IMPORT.txt` — **212 tracks**, zero
cross-installment duplicates. Each installment also has its own `.md` and `_IMPORT.txt`.

## The five installments

**1 — Classical & Folk Roots (the deep tradition).** `china_music_1_CLASSICAL_FOLK_ROOTS` (49). The
**pentatonic** foundation and the philosophy of sound; the scholar's **guqin** ("Flowing Water", on the
Voyager record); the **pipa**, **guzheng**, **erhu** (Abing's "Erquan Yingyue"), and wind instruments;
**Chinese opera** (Peking opera, Kunqu); ritual and court music (the Marquis Yi bells); Han regional
folk ("Jasmine Flower"); and the **55 minorities** (Mongolian throat-singing, Uyghur muqam, Tibetan).

**2 — Opera's Golden Age & the Road to the Republic (c. 1850-1949).**
`china_music_2_OPERA_GOLDEN_AGE_REPUBLIC` (41). Peking opera's peak and **Mei Lanfang**; the regional
operas; the fall of the Qing and the New Culture Movement; the **school-song** movement (Li Shutong's
"Song Bie"); the first modern composers; and music of national salvation (Nie Er's future national
anthem, Xian Xinghai's "Yellow River Cantata").

**3 — Shanghai Shidaiqu & the Mao Era (c. 1927-1978).** `china_music_3_SHIDAIQU_MAO_ERA` (41). The
Shanghai jazz age and **Zhou Xuan**'s "Golden Voice"; the seven great singing stars (Yao Lee's "Rose,
Rose, I Love You"); the 1949 ban on "yellow music" and the exodus to Hong Kong; the mass song ("The
East Is Red"); the "Butterfly Lovers" concerto; and the **Cultural Revolution**'s eight model works.

**4 — Cantopop & Mandopop: the Gangtai Golden Age (c. 1970-2000).**
`china_music_4_CANTOPOP_MANDOPOP` (40). **Teresa Teng**, the voice that "conquered" the mainland by
cassette; the birth of **Cantopop** (Sam Hui); the 1980s golden age (Leslie Cheung, Anita Mui); the
**Four Heavenly Kings**; Beyond and HK rock; Taiwan's campus folk (Lo Ta-yu) and Faye Wong; the karaoke
era.

**5 — The Mainland Finds Its Voice & Today (c. 1986-present).**
`china_music_5_MAINLAND_ROCK_CPOP_TODAY` (41). **Cui Jian** and the birth of Chinese rock ("Nothing to
My Name"); the 1990s Beijing underground; **Jay Chou**'s Mandopop revolution and "Chinese-style" pop;
the C-pop constellation; the folk/indie revival (Zhao Lei's "Chengdu"); the hip-hop explosion ("The Rap
of China", Higher Brothers); and the Douyin streaming age.

## Through-lines

- **The pentatonic soul.** From the guqin to Jay Chou's zhongguofeng, a pentatonic, gesture-rich
  aesthetic threads through 3,000 years and every genre — the deep continuity beneath the upheavals.
- **Music and the state.** No survey shows the political weight of music more starkly: Confucian ritual
  order, the school-song modernizers, revolutionary mass song, the Cultural Revolution's eight model
  works, and today's censorship all treat music as a matter of power. (Politically sensitive material is
  kept factual and brief.)
- **The three Chinas of song.** The 1949 divide split Chinese pop into a silenced mainland and a
  flourishing Hong Kong/Taiwan **gangtai** world — which then flooded back as the mainland reopened, and
  finally merged into one vast pan-Chinese pop market.
- **Tradition ↔ the West.** Chinese music repeatedly absorbs and resists foreign sound — Shanghai jazz,
  Soviet mass song, Cantopop's global gloss, Cui Jian's rock, Jay Chou's R&B — always re-Sinicizing it.
- **The East Asian family.** The pentatonic, silk-string world is shared with Japan and Korea (the
  surveys ahead), and Silk Road exchange ties it westward to Central Asia and Persia.

## Notes on method & scope

Named entries cite a canonical piece/song and performer; **bare titles** mark forms, instruments, or
artists best used as search seeds (the roots installments lean on these; the pop installments are more
named). All entries are transliterated (pinyin), ASCII-only. Deduplication is by **title only**, so
every title within the sub-region is unique. Scope: Hong Kong and Taiwan pop are included here as part
of the Chinese-language music world; Japan and Korea are separate East Asia surveys.
