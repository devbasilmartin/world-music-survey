# A Survey of American Music — The 1980s, Strand by Strand

The decade MTV rewired the industry (a hit now needed a look), hip-hop went from novelty to
serious art, metal and hardcore built fierce undergrounds, and synths + drum machines
reshaped pop and R&B. Two throughlines: (1) hip-hop's rise from 'The Message' to Public Enemy
and N.W.A -- the sound that takes over the 90s -- and (2) the video-era superstar (MJ/Prince/
Madonna) as a new kind of total-package artist. Underneath, the funk->hip-hop rhythm chain from
the 70s file is now driving the whole culture.

## THE MTV SUPERSTARS: THE VIDEO AGE

MTV launched in 1981 and rewired the industry: a hit now needed a look, not just a sound. Three American artists mastered the medium and became planetary forces. Michael Jackson's Thriller became the best-selling album ever; Prince fused funk, rock, and pop into a one-man revolution; Madonna turned reinvention itself into a career strategy.

- Billie Jean — Michael Jackson
- Beat It — Michael Jackson
- Thriller — Michael Jackson
- Wanna Be Startin' Somethin' — Michael Jackson
- When Doves Cry — Prince
- Purple Rain — Prince
- 1999 — Prince
- Kiss — Prince
- Like a Virgin — Madonna
- Like a Prayer — Madonna
- Into the Groove — Madonna

## HIP-HOP BECOMES AN ART FORM (Old School -> New School)

Rap grew up fast. Grandmaster Flash's 'The Message' proved hip-hop could be serious social commentary; Run-DMC stripped it to hard beats and rock guitar and crossed to MTV; LL Cool J and the Beastie Boys made it album-driven; and by decade's end it was the sound of a generation.

- The Message — Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five
- Planet Rock — Afrika Bambaataa and the Soulsonic Force
- Sucker M.C.'s — Run-DMC
- Walk This Way — Run-DMC and Aerosmith
- It's Like That — Run-DMC
- Rock Box — Run-DMC
- I Can't Live Without My Radio — LL Cool J
- Rock Hard — Beastie Boys
- Fight for Your Right — Beastie Boys
- Paul Revere — Beastie Boys
- Eric B. Is President — Eric B. & Rakim
- Paid in Full — Eric B. & Rakim

## CONSCIOUS & GANGSTA RAP EMERGE (late 80s)

By 1988 hip-hop split into its two most influential branches: Public Enemy weaponized it as dense, politically radical Black protest art, and N.W.A on the West Coast birthed gangsta rap — raw, controversial street reportage that would dominate the 90s.

- Fight the Power — Public Enemy
- Rebel Without a Pause — Public Enemy
- Bring the Noise — Public Enemy
- Straight Outta Compton — N.W.A
- F--- tha Police — N.W.A
- It Takes Two — Rob Base and DJ E-Z Rock
- Push It — Salt-N-Pepa
- Follow the Leader — Eric B. & Rakim

## SYNTH-POP & NEW WAVE (American branch)

Punk's arty younger sibling went electronic and danceable. Cheap synthesizers and drum machines made a sleek, modern pop that ruled MTV. The American new-wave scene fused it with funk (Talking Heads) and pure pop craft (the B-52's, Blondie's crossover).

- Once in a Lifetime — Talking Heads
- Burning Down the House — Talking Heads
- Rapture — Blondie
- Rock Lobster — The B-52's
- Whip It — Devo
- We Got the Beat — The Go-Go's
- Mexican Radio — Wall of Voodoo
- Genius of Love — Tom Tom Club

## HEARTLAND ROCK & THE ROCK MAINSTREAM

As synths took pop, a rootsy, blue-collar rock reasserted American guitar tradition. Springsteen's Born in the U.S.A. became a cultural monument; Mellencamp, Petty, Seger, and Bob Seger's peers kept the working-class narrative and the FM-radio anthem alive.

- Born in the U.S.A. — Bruce Springsteen
- Dancing in the Dark — Bruce Springsteen
- Jack & Diane — John Mellencamp
- Small Town — John Mellencamp
- The Boys of Summer — Don Henley
- Free Fallin' — Tom Petty
- I Won't Back Down — Tom Petty
- Old Time Rock and Roll — Bob Seger

## HARDCORE PUNK (the underground goes extreme)

Punk's American descendants pushed it faster, harder, and more political — building a fiercely independent DIY network of clubs and labels far from the mainstream. Hardcore is the underground bedrock that alternative rock and grunge grow out of.

- Rise Above — Black Flag
- Nervous Breakdown — Black Flag
- Holiday in Cambodia — Dead Kennedys
- Straight Edge — Minor Threat
- Institutionalized — Suicidal Tendencies
- Pay to Cum — Bad Brains
- Sonic Reducer — Dead Boys

## THRASH & THE METAL EXPLOSION

Metal split into a fast, aggressive underground (thrash — Metallica, Slayer) and a glossy, MTV-friendly mainstream (glam/hair metal). Both were huge; thrash's speed and darkness would prove the more lasting influence.

- Master of Puppets — Metallica
- One — Metallica
- Raining Blood — Slayer
- Peace Sells — Megadeth
- Shout at the Devil — Motley Crue
- Sweet Child o' Mine — Guns N' Roses
- Welcome to the Jungle — Guns N' Roses
- Every Rose Has Its Thorn — Poison
- Jump — Van Halen
- Panama — Van Halen

## R&B, QUIET STORM & THE NEW SOUL

R&B modernized with synths, drum machines, and slick production. Quincy Jones and others crafted a polished adult-contemporary soul; a young Whitney Houston became a vocal phenomenon; and the seeds of new jack swing (fusing R&B with hip-hop beats) were planted.

- Super Freak — Rick James
- I Wanna Dance with Somebody (Who Loves Me) — Whitney Houston
- How Will I Know — Whitney Houston
- Sexual Healing — Marvin Gaye
- Give Me the Night — George Benson
- Rock with You — Michael Jackson
- Control — Janet Jackson
- Nasty — Janet Jackson
- Poison — Bell Biv DeVoe

## POP CRAFT & THE ADULT-CONTEMPORARY CHARTS

The sound of 80s radio and film: big, produced, hook-driven pop and power ballads. Often dismissed later, but it was what most Americans actually heard on the radio and in every movie soundtrack of the decade.

- Girls Just Want to Have Fun — Cyndi Lauper
- Time After Time — Cyndi Lauper
- Footloose — Kenny Loggins
- Africa — Toto
- Jessie's Girl — Rick Springfield
- Broken Wings — Mr. Mister
- Eye of the Tiger — Survivor
- Sailing — Christopher Cross

## COUNTRY: NEO-TRADITIONALIST PUSHBACK

After the pop-country crossover of the late 70s (Urban Cowboy), a new generation pulled country back toward its honky-tonk roots — George Strait, Randy Travis, and Dwight Yoakam restored the twang, setting up the massive 90s country boom.

- Amarillo by Morning — George Strait
- All My Ex's Live in Texas — George Strait
- Forever and Ever, Amen — Randy Travis
- Guitars, Cadillacs — Dwight Yoakam
- Mama He's Crazy — The Judds
- 9 to 5 — Dolly Parton
