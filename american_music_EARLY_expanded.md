# A Survey of American Music — Early Era (pre-1890), EXPANDED

This supersedes Eras 1-4 of Part 1. ~75 tracks. Where a period-authentic recording
exists it's named; everything marked (Traditional) has no single authentic recording, so
search by title in YouTube Music and pick the version you like best.

Authenticity anchors used here:
- Colonial Williamsburg Fifes and Drums / Arthur F. Schrader (Smithsonian Folkways) — Revolutionary era, period instruments
- 2nd South Carolina String Band — antebellum & Civil War popular song on 1860s instruments
- Fisk Jubilee Singers — the authentic spiritual tradition (founded 1871)
- Sacred Harp Singers — shape-note style, essentially unchanged since the 1840s

## ERA 1 — Colonial & Indigenous Roots (pre-1770)

No recordings exist from this era, so these are the most period-faithful modern performances available. Two of America's three founding streams are audible here: the psalm-tunes and 'fuguing tunes' of New England Puritans (William Billings), and the Anglo-Celtic ballads colonists carried over. The third and in some ways most important stream — the music of enslaved Africans — was actively suppressed and unrecorded; you'll hear its fruit explode into view in Era 4 and everything after. NOTE ON INDIGENOUS MUSIC: Native music is a living oral tradition, not something a single 'period recording' can represent, and pre-contact song wasn't notated or recorded. Rather than drop in a token track, I've left it for a dedicated segment — say the word and I'll add a respectful set from Smithsonian Folkways / Canyon Records.

## ERA 2 — Revolution & Early Republic (1770–1820)

A new country reaches for a sound of its own. Fife-and-drum music carries the army; broadside ballads argue politics; and shape-note singing spreads through frontier churches — a rugged, open-throated congregational style that survives essentially unchanged today.

## ERA 3 — Antebellum, Minstrelsy & Parlor Song (1820–1860)

America's first true pop industry — and its most troubling. Blackface minstrelsy was the era's dominant popular entertainment: wildly popular, viciously racist, and the channel through which Black-derived rhythm and banjo first reached white audiences. I've represented the era through its still-performed survivors and Stephen Foster (the first great American songwriter) rather than its ugliest caricature songs like 'Jump Jim Crow' — historically pivotal, but not something to cue up for pleasure. The 2nd South Carolina String Band plays this repertoire on period instruments.

## ERA 4 — Civil War & the Rise of the Spiritual (1860–1890)

The war produces a torrent of song on both sides, and its aftermath brings the single most consequential development of the century: the Fisk Jubilee Singers carry the spiritual out of the plantation and into the world's concert halls, establishing the vocal and emotional DNA of gospel, blues, and soul. Out west, the cowboy song is born. The 2nd South Carolina String Band handles the camp songs; the Fisk Jubilee Singers, the spirituals.
