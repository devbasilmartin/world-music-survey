# The Story of European Music — Installment 4: The Romantic Era, Part 1 (c. 1815-1890)

Romanticism makes music maximally EMOTIONAL, personal, literary, and grand. Part 1 covers the
Western/Central European core. Throughlines: (1) the intimate German LIED (Schubert); (2) the
PIANO POETS turning the keyboard into everything from confession to spectacle (Chopin, Liszt);
(3) ITALIAN OPERA from bel canto to Verdi the national hero; (4) WAGNER reinventing opera as
music drama and cracking tonality open -- the pivot toward the 20th century; and (5) BRAHMS
carrying the pure-symphony tradition as Beethoven's heir. Part 2 next: the nationalists, the
Russians, and the late-Romantic giants.

## THE LIED & EARLY ROMANTICS (c. 1815-1850)

Romanticism turns music toward intense personal emotion, nature, the supernatural, and literature. The German art song (Lied) -- solo voice and piano setting poetry -- becomes a central intimate form, and Schubert is its supreme master, packing whole dramas into three minutes.

- Erlkonig — Franz Schubert
- Ave Maria (Ellens dritter Gesang) — Franz Schubert
- Symphony No. 8 (Unfinished): Allegro moderato — Franz Schubert
- Piano Quintet (Trout): Theme and Variations — Franz Schubert
- Dichterliebe: Ich grolle nicht — Robert Schumann
- Traumerei (Kinderszenen) — Robert Schumann
- A Midsummer Night's Dream: Wedding March — Felix Mendelssohn
- Violin Concerto in E minor: Allegro molto appassionato — Felix Mendelssohn
- Der Freischutz: Overture — Carl Maria von Weber

## THE PIANO POETS (c. 1830-1860)

The piano becomes the Romantic instrument par excellence -- capable of both whispered intimacy and thunderous virtuosity. Chopin makes it sing in an intensely personal, Polish-inflected voice; Liszt turns it into a one-man orchestra and invents the modern solo recital and the rock-star virtuoso (with Paganini doing the same on violin).

- Nocturne in E-flat major, Op. 9 No. 2 — Frederic Chopin
- Ballade No. 1 in G minor — Frederic Chopin
- Etude Op. 10 No. 3 (Tristesse) — Frederic Chopin
- Prelude in E minor, Op. 28 No. 4 — Frederic Chopin
- Fantaisie-Impromptu — Frederic Chopin
- Polonaise in A-flat major (Heroic) — Frederic Chopin
- Liebestraum No. 3 — Franz Liszt
- Hungarian Rhapsody No. 2 — Franz Liszt
- La Campanella — Franz Liszt
- Caprice No. 24 in A minor — Niccolo Paganini

## ITALIAN OPERA: BEL CANTO TO VERDI (c. 1815-1890)

Italian opera rules the theaters -- first the 'beautiful singing' of bel canto (Rossini, Bellini, Donizetti), then Verdi, who becomes a symbol of Italian national unity, writing operas of tremendous dramatic force and unforgettable melody that are still the heart of the repertoire.

- The Barber of Seville: Largo al factotum — Gioachino Rossini
- William Tell: Overture — Gioachino Rossini
- Norma: Casta Diva — Vincenzo Bellini
- Lucia di Lammermoor: Il dolce suono (Mad Scene) — Gaetano Donizetti
- Rigoletto: La donna e mobile — Giuseppe Verdi
- La Traviata: Libiamo ne' lieti calici (Brindisi) — Giuseppe Verdi
- Aida: Triumphal March — Giuseppe Verdi
- Nabucco: Va, pensiero (Chorus of the Hebrew Slaves) — Giuseppe Verdi
- Messa da Requiem: Dies Irae — Giuseppe Verdi

## WAGNER & GERMAN MUSIC DRAMA (c. 1840-1880)

Richard Wagner reinvents opera as 'music drama' -- continuous, myth-driven, built on recurring leitmotifs, and harmonically revolutionary. His 'Tristan chord' cracks tonality open and points straight at the 20th century. Monumentally influential, endlessly controversial, and the pivot point of Romantic music.

- Die Walkure: Ride of the Valkyries — Richard Wagner
- Tristan und Isolde: Prelude and Liebestod — Richard Wagner
- Lohengrin: Bridal Chorus — Richard Wagner
- Tannhauser: Overture — Richard Wagner
- Das Rheingold: Entry of the Gods into Valhalla — Richard Wagner
- Die Meistersinger von Nurnberg: Overture — Richard Wagner

## THE SYMPHONIC TRADITION: BRAHMS & ABSOLUTE MUSIC (c. 1850-1890)

Against Wagner's radical drama, Brahms carries the 'absolute music' torch -- pure symphonies and chamber works of Classical rigor and Romantic warmth, seen as Beethoven's true heir. Bruckner builds vast, cathedral-like symphonies of spiritual grandeur. This is the conservative-but-profound mainstream of German music.

- Symphony No. 1 in C minor: Finale — Johannes Brahms
- Symphony No. 3: Poco allegretto — Johannes Brahms
- Hungarian Dance No. 5 — Johannes Brahms
- Violin Concerto in D major: Finale — Johannes Brahms
- A German Requiem: How Lovely Is Thy Dwelling Place — Johannes Brahms
- Wiegenlied (Brahms Lullaby) — Johannes Brahms
- Symphony No. 7: Adagio — Anton Bruckner

## FRENCH ROMANTICISM (c. 1830-1880)

France develops its own color-forward, theatrical Romanticism -- Berlioz's wildly imaginative orchestration, Bizet's earthy realism, Saint-Saens' elegance, and Offenbach's champagne wit. A distinctly French sensibility that will lead straight into Impressionism.

- Symphonie Fantastique: March to the Scaffold — Hector Berlioz
- Carmen: Habanera — Georges Bizet
- Carmen: Overture — Georges Bizet
- Danse Macabre — Camille Saint-Saens
- The Carnival of the Animals: The Swan — Camille Saint-Saens
- Orpheus in the Underworld: Infernal Galop (Can-Can) — Jacques Offenbach
- Thais: Meditation — Jules Massenet
