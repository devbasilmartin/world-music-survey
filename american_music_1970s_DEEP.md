# A Survey of American Music — The 1970s, Strand by Strand

The decade the 60s streams professionalize and fragment into distinct scenes. Funk becomes
an ecosystem; soul turns conscious then cinematic; the singer-songwriters go inward; disco
builds from funk+gospel on the dancefloor and centers the DJ; punk resets rock; salsa surges
from the barrios; outlaw country rebels against Nashville; and in the Bronx, hip-hop is born.
Two throughlines to follow: (1) the FUNK -> DISCO -> HIP-HOP rhythm chain that sets up the next
40 years, and (2) rock splitting into mellow-California vs. loud-Southern vs. stripped-punk.

## FUNK MATURES INTO AN ECOSYSTEM

James Brown's late-60s invention becomes a whole genre. Sly & the Family Stone add psychedelia and racial-integration idealism; George Clinton's Parliament-Funkadelic build a cosmic mythology out of pure groove; Earth, Wind & Fire fuse funk with jazz, gospel positivity, and showmanship. This is rhythm as the organizing principle, and it's the direct DNA of disco and hip-hop.

## SOCIALLY CONSCIOUS & CINEMATIC SOUL

Motown and its peers grew up. Marvin Gaye and Stevie Wonder seized creative control and made album-length statements about war, ecology, and the inner city; Curtis Mayfield and Isaac Hayes scored blaxploitation films, turning soul into cinematic suites. Philadelphia International (Gamble & Huff) built the lush 'Philly soul' that became disco's blueprint.

## THE SINGER-SONGWRITERS TURN INWARD

Dylan's 1965 revolution matures into a confessional, personal genre. Laurel Canyon becomes the center of a warm, introspective sound built on acoustic craft and emotional candor — the soundtrack of the post-60s comedown.

## COUNTRY ROCK & THE CALIFORNIA SOUND

Gram Parsons' dream of fusing country and rock takes over Southern California. The Eagles smooth it into the biggest American sound of the decade; Linda Ronstadt makes it a hit machine. This is the mellow, harmony-rich radio staple that defined 70s FM.

- Take It Easy — Eagles
- Hotel California — Eagles
- Peaceful Easy Feeling — Eagles
- You're No Good — Linda Ronstadt

- Midnight Rider — The Allman Brothers Band

## SOUTHERN ROCK & HARD ROCK

A rootsier, bluesier, guitar-heavy rock rises from the South (Allmans, Skynyrd) while hard rock and the seeds of metal get heavier and more theatrical. This is the loud, blue-collar counterweight to the mellow California sound.

## DISCO: THE DANCEFLOOR TAKES OVER

Built from funk's groove, Philly soul's lush strings, and gospel's ecstatic vocals, disco was music engineered for the club and the DJ. Born in Black, Latino, and gay underground clubs in NYC, it conquered the mainstream and, crucially, put the DJ and the extended beat at the center — a direct setup for hip-hop and electronic dance music.

- I Feel Love — Donna Summer
- Love to Love You Baby — Donna Summer
- Le Freak — Chic
- Good Times — Chic
- Get Down Tonight — KC and the Sunshine Band

- Y.M.C.A. — Village People
- Don't Leave Me This Way — Thelma Houston
- I Will Survive — Gloria Gaynor

## PUNK: STRIPPING IT BACK (NYC then everywhere)

A reaction against bloated arena rock and disco's polish. Starting at CBGB in New York, punk stripped rock to raw speed, three chords, and attitude. It reset rock's clock and spawned the entire alternative/indie tradition that follows.

- Blitzkrieg Bop — Ramones
- Sheena Is a Punk Rocker — Ramones
- Gloria — Patti Smith
- Marquee Moon — Television
- Psycho Killer — Talking Heads
- Personality Crisis — New York Dolls
- Blank Generation — Richard Hell and the Voidoids

## THE BIRTH OF HIP-HOP (Bronx, 1973-1979)

The single most consequential musical birth since rock. At Bronx block parties, DJ Kool Herc isolated and looped the 'breakbeat'; Grandmaster Flash refined the turntable techniques; Afrika Bambaataa built a culture around it; and in 1979 'Rapper's Delight' put rap on vinyl and into the mainstream. Everything in modern pop rhythm traces back here.

- Rapper's Delight — The Sugarhill Gang
- Superrappin' — Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five
- King Tim III (Personality Jock) — Fatback Band
- Christmas Rappin' — Kurtis Blow

## SALSA & THE LATIN EXPLOSION (Nuyorican NYC)

Puerto Rican and Cuban musicians in New York fused Cuban son, mambo, and jazz into salsa, powered by the Fania label. It became the pulse of Latino America and a permanent thread in the US musical fabric.

- El Cantante — Hector Lavoe
- Pedro Navaja — Ruben Blades and Willie Colon
- Quimbara — Celia Cruz and Johnny Pacheco
- Que Bien Te Ves — Fania All-Stars
- Vamonos Pa'l Monte — Eddie Palmieri

## OUTLAW COUNTRY & COUNTRY'S MAINSTREAM

A rebellion against Nashville's slick assembly line. Willie Nelson and Waylon Jennings took creative control, grew their hair out, and made rawer, rootsier records — while Dolly Parton and others ruled the mainstream and crossed over to pop.

- On the Road Again — Willie Nelson
- Blue Eyes Crying in the Rain — Willie Nelson
- Are You Sure Hank Done It This Way — Waylon Jennings
- Jolene — Dolly Parton

- The Devil Went Down to Georgia — The Charlie Daniels Band

## JAZZ FUSION & THE SPIRITUAL/GROOVE BRANCHES

Miles Davis plugged jazz into rock rhythms and electric instruments, birthing fusion. Others pushed jazz toward funk grooves or spiritual modal explorations. Jazz stopped being the mainstream but became a laboratory feeding everything else.

- Birdland — Weather Report
- Chameleon — Herbie Hancock
- Spain — Chick Corea and Return to Forever
- Red Clay — Freddie Hubbard
- Mister Magic — Grover Washington Jr.

## PROG, ART ROCK & THE ROCK MAINSTREAM

Ambitious album-oriented rock: complex song suites, studio craft, and stadium-scale songwriting became the FM-radio center. The polished, professional rock that punk was rebelling against — and that most listeners actually bought.
