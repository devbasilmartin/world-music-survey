# A Survey of American Music — Part 1

A chronological listen through the story of American popular music, from colonial
recreations to the streaming era. Roughly 300 tracks across 15 eras. This is Part 1 of
a build toward ~1,000 — every era can be expanded much deeper.

The through-line: American music is a series of fusions, born mostly from the meeting of
African, European, and Indigenous traditions. Listen for how each era's new sound is last
era's streams braided together.

## ERA 1 — Colonial & Indigenous Roots (pre-1770) [modern recreations]

Nothing here was recorded at the time — these are modern performances standing in for the traditions. Three streams start flowing: Indigenous song, the psalms and 'fuguing tunes' of English and New England Puritans, and the Anglo-Celtic ballads and fiddle tunes that colonists carried over. Findability note: the Indigenous and colonial entries may need manual searching in the import.

- Wondrous Love — Sacred Harp Singers
- Idumea — Sacred Harp Singers at Liberty Church
- Pretty Saro — Iris DeMent

- Cherokee Morning Song — Walela

## ERA 2 — Revolution & Early Republic (1770–1820) [recreations]

A new nation looks for a sound. Shape-note singing spreads through rural churches, patriotic songs take shape, and the seeds of the spiritual are sown in the fields of the enslaved South.

- Yankee Doodle — United States Army Band
- The Star-Spangled Banner — Fife and Drum Corps
- Simple Gifts — Aaron Copland
- Wayfaring Stranger — Emmylou Harris
- Poor Wayfaring Stranger — Jos Slovick
- Bright Morning Stars — Alison Krauss

## ERA 3 — Antebellum, Minstrelsy & Parlor Song (1820–1860) [recreations]

America's first pop-song industry arrives — much of it through blackface minstrelsy, a hugely popular and deeply racist form whose tunes nonetheless carried Black-derived rhythm into the mainstream. Stephen Foster becomes the country's first great professional songwriter, and Appalachian balladry deepens.

- Oh! Susanna — James Taylor
- Hard Times Come Again No More — Mavis Staples
- Beautiful Dreamer — Roberta Flack
- Camptown Races — Al Jolson
- My Old Kentucky Home — Johnny Cash
- Wildwood Flower — The Carter Family
- Man of Constant Sorrow — The Stanley Brothers
- Down in the Valley — Solomon Burke

## ERA 4 — Civil War, Spirituals & Reconstruction (1860–1890) [recreations + earliest recordings]

War songs sweep both sides, and after emancipation the Fisk Jubilee Singers carry the spiritual to concert halls and the world — the first great flowering of a distinctly African American art form, and a taproot for gospel, blues, and soul.

- Battle Hymn of the Republic — Mormon Tabernacle Choir

- Go Down Moses — Paul Robeson
- Steal Away — Fisk Jubilee Singers
- Deep River — Marian Anderson

## ERA 5 — The Recording Era Begins: Ragtime, Marches & Early Blues (1890–1917)

Sound recording arrives, and American music can finally be heard as it was made. Scott Joplin's ragtime syncopation sweeps the nation, Sousa's marches define civic sound, W.C. Handy publishes the blues, and Tin Pan Alley industrializes songwriting.

## ERA 6 — The Jazz Age & the First Blues Records (1917–1929)

Jazz erupts out of New Orleans and up the Mississippi to Chicago; the first jazz and blues records ignite a craze. Louis Armstrong invents the modern soloist. Meanwhile the first country records are cut in the rural South.

## ERA 7 — Depression, Delta Blues & the Swing of the 1930s

The Delta blues of Robert Johnson and Son House distills a whole tradition; Woody Guthrie chronicles the Dust Bowl; and swing becomes America's mainstream pop as big bands rule the dance halls. Gospel is born from the blues in Thomas Dorsey's hands.

## ERA 8 — Bebop, Jump Blues & the Birth of Modern Country (1940s)

Charlie Parker and Dizzy Gillespie turn jazz into a fast, cerebral art (bebop). Louis Jordan's jump blues and the electric guitar of T-Bone Walker point straight at rock and roll. Hank Williams gives country its aching modern voice.
