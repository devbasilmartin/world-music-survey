# The Story of Southern African Music — Complete Master Guide

A chronological survey of how the music of Southern Africa evolved, from the San healing-dance and the
Shona mbira to the amapiano and Grammy-winning Afropop that top today's charts. **249 tracks, from
humanity's oldest song to the streaming present, one continuous story** — and a companion to the West
Africa, United States, and Latin American surveys it constantly touches.

**The one idea that runs through everything:** Southern Africa's music is a story of collision under
pressure. The oldest layer on Earth (San, Khoi) and sophisticated bow, overtone, and mbira traditions
met the Christian mission hymn and the migrant-labour mine compound, and out of that grinding
machinery — colonialism, segregation, apartheid — the region forged one brilliant urban style after
another: marabi, kwela, township jazz, mbaqanga, isicathamiya, chimurenga, bubblegum, kwaito, and
amapiano. Again and again, music made under oppression became a weapon, a memory, and finally a global
export. And, as in West Africa, the current now runs outward: the world dances to Johannesburg.

## The Arc (continuous narrative)

**The deep-traditional layer.** The musical bow (Zulu ugubhu, Xhosa uhadi), Xhosa overtone/split-tone
singing, the trance-inducing Shona mbira, the San healing dance (perhaps the oldest continuous music
on Earth), the Chopi xylophone orchestras — and, already reshaping them, the mission hymn and the mine
compound.

**Marabi, early recording & mission choralism (c. 1900–1948).** The slumyard keyboard cycle marabi
(South Africa's ragtime), Solomon Linda's 1939 "Mbube" (stolen into "The Lion Sleeps Tonight"), Reuben
Caluza's protest choral songs, and the marabi-to-jazz bridge.

**Kwela, sax jive & township jazz (c. 1950–1962).** The Sophiatown golden age: Spokes Mashiyane's
pennywhistle kwela, the Manhattan Brothers, the young Miriam Makeba, the Jazz Epistles (Abdullah
Ibrahim, Masekela) — bulldozed by the 1955 removals and scattered into exile.

**Mbaqanga, isicathamiya & the Soweto sound (c. 1960–1979).** The electric mbaqanga groove
(Mahlathini & the Mahotella Queens), isicathamiya (Ladysmith Black Mambazo), maskandi, and the 70s
soul crossover — defiant Black pop under high apartheid.

**Mbira, chimurenga & the liberation era (c. 1970–1989).** Thomas Mapfumo's electric-mbira chimurenga
and the Zimbabwe war, Oliver Mtukudzi, the Bhundu Boys' jit, Zambian Zamrock, Mozambican marrabenta,
and Juluka crossing apartheid's colour line — music as a weapon of liberation.

**Graceland, bubblegum & the birth of kwaito (c. 1985–2000).** The Graceland global spotlight and its
boycott controversy, Brenda Fassie's bubblegum reign, Lucky Dube's reggae, and the post-1994 birth of
kwaito — the first genre wholly of the free South Africa.

**Amapiano & the global present (c. 2000–present).** Black Coffee's world-conquering house, Durban
gqom, the amapiano log-drum explosion, "Jerusalema" as a global dance challenge, and Tyla's
Grammy-winning "Water" — Southern African music AS global pop.

## The braid (the streams that run through the whole story)

- **The traditional layer** — bow/overtone/mbira/San (I1) surfaces again and again: mbira → chimurenga
  guitar (I5); Xhosa tradition → Thandiswa Mazwai (I7); maskandi from migrant string music (I2) →
  its modern revival (I4, I7).
- **The choral/vocal line** — mission makwaya (I2) → isicathamiya and Ladysmith (I4) → Graceland's
  global stage (I6).
- **The urban-dance line** — marabi (I2) → kwela and township jazz (I3) → mbaqanga (I4) → bubblegum
  and kwaito (I6) → gqom and amapiano (I7): each generation's new ratio of the same ingredients.
- **The struggle line** — Caluza's Land Act songs (I2) → the exile jazz of Sophiatown (I3) →
  chimurenga and Juluka (I5) → kwaito's "born-free" swagger (I6).
