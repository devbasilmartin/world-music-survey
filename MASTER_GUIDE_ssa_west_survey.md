# The Story of West African Music — Complete Master Guide

A chronological survey of how the music of West Africa evolved, from the griot's kora and the Sundiata
epic (c. 1235) to the Afrobeats that top today's global charts. **256 tracks, ~800 years, one
continuous story** — and the deep rhythmic taproot of both the United States and Latin American
surveys.

**The one idea that runs through everything:** West Africa is the source. Through the transatlantic
slave trade, its rhythmic and vocal DNA became the engine of music across the Americas — the banjo,
the blues, the clave, the batá — and yet the home tradition kept evolving on its own terms, absorbing
the guitar, jazz horns, funk, and finally digital pop, and re-exporting each in its own accent. The
story runs **oral griot tradition → palm-wine guitar → highlife → juju/fuji → Afrobeat → the Mande
world-music era → global Afrobeats**, and at the end the current fully reverses: for the first time,
the world dances to Lagos and Accra.

## The Arc (continuous narrative)

**Griot / Mande roots (the deep-traditional layer).** West Africa has no ancient notation; its deepest
layer is oral, carried by the hereditary MANDE JELI (griot) — historian-praise-singers whose kora,
balafon, and ngoni have kept the memory of the Mali empire since Sundiata (c. 1235). Beyond the Mande
lie the Wolof, Fula, Hausa, Yoruba, and Akan traditions. This is the layer that crossed the Atlantic:
akonting → banjo, Mande pentatonic → blues, Yoruba orisha drums → batá and candomblé.

**Palm-wine guitar & the early recording era (c. 1900–1950).** The Kru mariners carry the guitar ashore
and a new popular music is born — Sierra Leone maringa, the Gold Coast's osibisaaba and adaha, and the
first Lagos juju — captured on the first Zonophone and HMV 78s, and threaded to the Caribbean by the
returnee gumbe networks of the Black Atlantic.

**Highlife's golden age (c. 1950–1969).** With independence (Ghana, 1957), HIGHLIFE becomes the
continent's most sophisticated pop: E.T. Mensah's dance bands, the guitar-band concert party, and a
Nigerian scene from Bobby Benson's Lagos to the gorgeous Igbo highlife of the east — the latter cut
down by the Biafran war.

**Juju & fuji explode (c. 1965–1980).** Yoruba country becomes the engine room: I.K. Dairo, King Sunny
Adé, and Ebenezer Obey turn JUJU into spectacle, FUJI is born from Ramadan were-music, and a
Congo-inflected guitar pop yields the best-selling African single ever, 'Sweet Mother'.

**Afrobeat & Afro-funk (c. 1968–1980).** Fela Kuti fuses Yoruba rhythm with James Brown's funk to
invent AFROBEAT — half-hour grooves of scalding political satire, driven by Tony Allen — and pays for
it under state violence. Around him, a whole Nigerian and Ghanaian funk-rock underground catches fire.

**The Mande superstars & the world-music era (c. 1980–2000).** West Africa conquers the global stage:
Salif Keita's golden voice, Mory Kanté's electric kora, Youssou N'Dour's mbalax, Ali Farka Touré's
desert blues (the clearest proof the blues was partly Malian), and Oumou Sangaré's Wassoulou feminism.

**Modern Afrobeats & the global takeover (c. 2000–present).** After a from-zero digital rebirth,
AFROBEATS goes worldwide — Wizkid, Davido, and Burna Boy fill arenas and win Grammys; Rema, CKay, and
Tems top the world charts; Ghana's hiplife and azonto run alongside. Burna Boy samples his
grandfather's client Fela, closing the loop.

## The braid (the streams that run through the whole story)

- **The griot/Mande lineage** — kora and jeli song (I1) → the Rail Band orchestras (I3/I6) → Salif
  Keita, Toumani Diabaté, Oumou Sangaré on the world stage (I6). The oldest tradition became a global
  pop career without losing its language.
- **The guitar line** — Kru palm-wine picking (I2) → highlife guitar bands (I3) → juju's massed
  guitars and pedal steel (I4) → Afro-rock (I5) → the digital guitars of Afrobeats (I7).
- **The Black-Atlantic rhythm** — orisha and griot roots (I1) → gumbe returnees (I2) → the highlife
  "Latin tinge" (I3) → Fela's funk (I5) → Afrobeats over Drake and Beyoncé beats (I7). The engine that
  built the Americas, feeding back the other way.
